First open MT pipeline and parallel corpus for Tunisian Darija in Arabizi

News time: 2026-07-05T18:08:49+00:00 · Source: reddit

An 18-year-old independent researcher from Tunisia has launched a foundational NLP resource for Tunisian Darija — a widely spoken but critically under-resourced Arabic dialect — by building the first open, from-scratch machine translation (MT) pipeline and parallel corpus specifically designed for its Arabizi orthography. Arabizi refers to the informal, Latin-based writing system used across North Africa, where numerals like '3', '7', '9', and '5' substitute for Arabic letters with similar phonetic values (e.g., '3' for ع, '7' for ح). Unlike Modern Standard Arabic (MSA), Tunisian Darija diverges significantly in grammar, vocabulary, and pronunciation — and existing Arabic NLP tools fail catastrophically when applied to Arabizi text due to orthographic mismatch and lack of dialect-specific training data.

The project features a custom SentencePiece Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenizer explicitly engineered to treat Arabizi numerals as protected symbols — ensuring they remain intact during subword segmentation rather than being split or misinterpreted. The underlying Transformer model is a 15.6-million-parameter encoder-decoder architecture trained entirely from scratch: first transfer-learned on cleaned Moroccan Darija data (a closely related dialect), then fine-tuned on just 553 hand-crafted Tunisian–English sentence pairs. Every component — preprocessing scripts, training configuration, evaluation metrics, and documentation — is fully open-source and reproducible.

Current performance stands at a BLEU score of 3.89 on a small, locked test set — a deliberately transparent baseline reflecting the acute data scarcity, not architectural weakness. The creator openly acknowledges that scaling high-quality, ethically sourced data is the central challenge. To address this, they are expanding the effort into a community-driven, consent-aware curation initiative: field-collected utterances will be documented with provenance tags, contributor consent, and linguistic validation. The goal is not just more data, but trusted data — built with transparency, accountability, and dialectal authenticity at its core.

This work bridges multiple gaps simultaneously: it challenges the MSA-centric bias in Arabic NLP, demonstrates how transfer learning across closely related dialects can bootstrap low-resource MT, and models ethical, participatory corpus development for marginalized language varieties. For linguists, developers, and speakers alike, it transforms Tunisian Darija from an ‘invisible’ dialect in AI systems into a viable, community-owned target for technological inclusion.
