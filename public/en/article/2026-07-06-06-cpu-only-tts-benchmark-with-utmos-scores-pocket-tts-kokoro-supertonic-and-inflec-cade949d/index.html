CPU-Only TTS Benchmark with UTMOS Scores: Pocket TTS, Kokoro, Supertonic, and Inflect-Nano Compared

News time: 2026-07-06T15:17:36+00:00 · Source: reddit

A new, rigorously designed CPU-only text-to-speech (TTS) benchmark has emerged—offering rare, reproducible insights into how lightweight TTS models perform under real-world constrained hardware. Conducted on an Intel Xeon 8272CL (4 cores, 15.6GB RAM) with CUDA explicitly disabled, the evaluation pits four distinct architectures against one another: Kyutai’s novel streaming LM-based Pocket TTS (~100M params), Kokoro 82M (StyleTTS2-inspired, available in both PyTorch and ONNX), Supertonic 3 (with 2- and 5-step flow-matching variants), and Inflect-Nano-v1 (a compact 4.6M FastSpeech-style model). Each configuration ran six text lengths—from 12 to 1712 characters—with five timed repetitions per cell after discarding a warmup run, totaling 180 independent inference measurements.

Quality was assessed objectively using UTMOS (utmos22_strong), a neural MOS predictor trained to emulate human listening scores; speed was measured via Real-Time Factor (RTF), where lower values indicate faster synthesis relative to audio duration. The results reveal surprising architectural trade-offs: Pocket TTS achieved the highest RTF (0.714) and remarkably flat scaling—its RTF varied only between 0.69 and 0.76 across all text lengths—thanks to its autoregressive, streaming token emission over Kyutai’s Mimi neural audio codec. In contrast, Kokoro’s RTF climbed from 0.49 to 0.83 as input length increased, while Supertonic’s dropped from 0.36 to 0.20, reflecting divergent fixed-overhead behaviors.

UTMOS scores further exposed metric limitations: Inflect-Nano scored only 3.48—deceptively mid-tier—despite sounding distinctly buzzy and robotic. This aligns with known UTMOS biases toward clean HiFi-GAN vocoder outputs, even when prosody is unnatural. Pocket TTS scored 4.10 and sounded subjectively more natural, underscoring that a single MOS number cannot disentangle cleanliness from naturalness. Meanwhile, Inflect-Nano’s undocumented 14.93-second output cap (from max_frames = 1400) artificially inflated its throughput on longer texts—a critical caveat for fair comparison.

Another notable finding concerns deployment pragmatics: Kokoro’s ONNX runtime outperformed PyTorch on Intel Xeon (0.641 vs. 0.665 RTF), reversing prior results seen on AMD EPYC—highlighting how CPU vendor-specific kernel optimizations meaningfully impact model ranking. And beyond the benchmark axes, Pocket TTS uniquely supports zero-shot voice cloning from just ~5 seconds of reference audio—a capability absent in all other models tested but omitted from scoring to ensure fairness. This underscores a broader truth: RTF-and-MOS benchmarks, while valuable, inherently miss capabilities that are asymmetric across models.
