Practitioner Seeks Best LLMs and Public Datasets for AI Red-Teaming

News time: 2026-07-05T21:49:16+00:00 · Source: reddit

A machine learning practitioner recently posted a high-signal question on Reddit’s r/MachineLearning forum, asking for empirically grounded recommendations on large language models (LLMs) best suited for generating adversarial red-team attacks—and for publicly available, high-quality benchmark datasets to evaluate AI agent security. The query reflects a growing operational need in AI safety: as organizations deploy increasingly complex LLM-powered agents, robust, automated red-teaming has shifted from theoretical research to engineering necessity.

The user specifically seeks models capable of crafting diverse, realistic attack vectors—including jailbreaks, SQL injection, indirect prompt injection, tool misuse, multi-turn exploits, and prompt leakage—across both closed- and open-source options. They emphasize realism and challenge: not just syntactic novelty, but attacks that reliably bypass real-world safeguards like moderation layers or function-calling constraints. This goes beyond simple toxicity testing; it targets the emergent attack surface of agentic workflows where planning, tool orchestration, and stateful dialogue create new failure modes.

On the data side, the request highlights a critical gap: while dozens of benchmarks exist for general LLM capabilities (e.g., MMLU, HumanEval), few are purpose-built for agent-level security. The ideal dataset would be a curated, human-validated “golden set” of adversarial prompts—each labeled by attack type, success rate against common defenses, and contextual constraints—enabling reproducible, apples-to-apples evaluation across different red-teaming pipelines.

Several community-maintained resources now address these needs. Promptfoo offers an open-source red-teaming framework with modular prompt generation and scoring. GitHub repositories like awesome-agent-benchmarks and ai-agent-benchmark-compendium catalog evaluation suites spanning tool use, multi-turn reasoning, and domain-specific threats (e.g., CBRNE). Most notably, the Open Agent Security Benchmark (OASB) positions itself as a vendor-neutral standard—grounded in real-world incident data and designed for compliance, behavioral governance, and tool-level robustness testing.

Ultimately, this question underscores how AI security is maturing: moving from ad-hoc, model-specific probing toward standardized, agent-aware red-teaming infrastructure. Choosing the right generator model isn’t just about raw capability—it’s about alignment with threat modeling, controllability, and integration into CI/CD pipelines. Likewise, adopting a shared benchmark like OASB helps align industry expectations and accelerates collective defense.
