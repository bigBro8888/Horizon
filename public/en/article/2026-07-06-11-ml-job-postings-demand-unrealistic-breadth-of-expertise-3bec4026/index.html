ML Job Postings Demand Unrealistic Breadth of Expertise

News time: 2026-07-06T18:31:54.905042+00:00 · Source: reddit

A recent Reddit post has struck a chord across the machine learning community: a practitioner describes scrolling through industrial ML job listings and being stunned by the sheer scope—and apparent impossibility—of their requirements. One non-FAANG, non-AI-lab company seeking talent for robot learning demanded deep expertise in large language models (LLMs), vision–language–action models (VLAs), vision–language models (VLMs), and action transformers—all alongside mastery of robotic dynamics, kinematics, sensor fusion, model predictive control, reinforcement learning, CUDA programming, FPGA acceleration, C++23, Python3 best practices, and top-tier conference publications. The post notes that even niche tools like 'RLib'—a simulator not widely recognized—were listed as required familiarity.

The author draws an evocative analogy to Terence Tao’s observation about mathematical specialization: just as world-class analysts and algebraists rarely overlap due to fundamentally different modes of thinking and infinite depth in each domain, expecting one engineer to possess deep expertise across robotics, foundation models, hardware acceleration, and modern software engineering is akin to asking for a mythical hybrid role—one that doesn’t exist in practice. This isn’t about high standards; it’s about conflating aspirational wishlists with realistic hiring criteria.

What makes this trend especially concerning is its pervasiveness beyond elite labs. While FAANG or Anthropic might credibly seek such rare polymaths, the post highlights that mid-tier industrial automation firms—whose core products may involve modest ML integration—are now copying elite job templates wholesale. These listings often demand 3–5 years of ‘non-academic’ experience while simultaneously requiring publication records typical of PhD researchers—a contradiction that signals either poor role definition or reliance on automated keyword-scanning ATS systems.

The implications extend beyond candidate frustration. Over-specified roles inflate time-to-hire, widen equity gaps (disproportionately excluding career-changers, self-taught engineers, and underrepresented groups), and misalign teams around ill-defined responsibilities. Meanwhile, practitioners report seeing similar patterns across sectors—from automotive to logistics—suggesting a systemic issue rooted in hype-driven hiring, miscommunication between HR and engineering leads, and the uncritical adoption of AI buzzwords without technical grounding. The real need isn’t omniscient unicorns—it’s clear role scoping, realistic skill mapping, and investment in cross-disciplinary upskilling.
