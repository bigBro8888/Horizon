Ph.D. Thesis Introduces Production-Ready Differentiable Ray Tracing for Radio Propagation

News time: 2026-07-08T04:29:38.096369+00:00 · Source: reddit

A groundbreaking Ph.D. thesis by Julien Eertmans introduces DiffeRT — a fully differentiable, JAX-based ray tracing framework tailored for radio propagation modeling in wireless communications. Rather than assembling prior publications, the thesis is structured as a self-contained, textbook-style resource — divided into three intuitive parts: Understanding (foundational physics of electromagnetics and geometrical optics), Building (GPU-accelerated path tracing with novel discontinuity-smoothing techniques to ensure stable gradients), and Using (practical inverse applications like channel estimation, material parameter calibration, and ML-assisted generative path sampling). This design bridges a critical gap between high-fidelity physical simulation and modern machine learning workflows.

The core innovation lies in enabling exact gradient computation through complex, real-world radio environments — something previously hindered by non-differentiable rendering bottlenecks and numerical approximations. By embedding automatic differentiation directly into the ray tracing pipeline using JAX’s functional paradigm, the framework supports gradient-based optimization without surrogate models or finite-difference hacks. This unlocks rigorous inverse design: for instance, inferring building material properties from measured signal strength, or optimizing antenna placement to meet coverage targets — all via end-to-end differentiable simulation.

Crucially, the work prioritizes reproducibility and open science. The thesis includes a complete open-source implementation (DiffeRT2d on GitHub), with TeX source files publicly archived. It builds heavily on Patrick Kidger’s JAX ecosystem — especially jaxtyping, equinox, and optimistix — demonstrating how mature ML infrastructure can be repurposed for scientific computing beyond neural networks. Unlike prior differentiable renderers focused on computer graphics (e.g., PyTorch-based DiffRT for inverse rendering), DiffeRT targets the distinct challenges of RF physics: longer wavelengths, diffraction effects, and macro-scale urban environments.

This effort exemplifies the rising paradigm of differentiable simulation in ML-for-science. As next-generation wireless systems (6G, integrated sensing & communication) demand co-design of hardware, environment, and algorithms, tools that unify physics fidelity with gradient-based learning become indispensable. Eertmans’ thesis doesn’t just present results — it delivers pedagogy, software, and a replicable methodology, inviting researchers across electromagnetics, optimization, and ML to collaborate on inverse problems grounded in first principles.
