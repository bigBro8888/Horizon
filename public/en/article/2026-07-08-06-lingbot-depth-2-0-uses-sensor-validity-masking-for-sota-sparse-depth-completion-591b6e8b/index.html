LingBot-Depth 2.0 Uses Sensor-Validity Masking for SOTA Sparse Depth Completion

News time: 2026-07-08T04:29:38.096810+00:00 · Source: reddit

LingBot-Depth 2.0, developed by Robbyant—an embodied AI team under Ant Group—introduces a paradigm shift in masked depth modeling: instead of applying synthetic random masks during training, the model leverages actual sensor-invalid regions as natural supervision signals. These include areas where RGB-D cameras fail—such as specular highlights on metal, transparent surfaces like glass or acrylic, and textureless walls—precisely mirroring real-world deployment conditions. By treating these physically grounded failure modes as the sole masking criterion, the model learns to reconstruct depth where it matters most, directly optimizing for robustness against sensor limitations rather than abstract patch corruption.

The architecture retains the same masked depth modeling (MDM) pipeline as version 1.0 but upgrades two key components: encoder initialization and data scale. Specifically, LingBot-Vision—a self-supervised vision foundation model—replaces prior backbones as the ViT-L and ViT-g encoder initialization. A rigorously controlled ablation study confirms this change alone drives most gains: with identical training recipes, data curation, and masking logic, swapping to LingBot-Vision yields superior RMSE across nearly all benchmarks. Notably, DINOv2 remains slightly stronger only on Hammer-captured scenes—suggesting domain-specific pretraining advantages persist in certain hardware-conditioned regimes.

Performance results are striking: LingBot-Depth 2.0 achieves the best reported RMSE on 7 out of 8 standard block-masked and sparse-depth benchmarks—including DIODE-Indoor, where its RMSE drops nearly in half versus v1.0—and ranks first on 6 of 8 real-camera configurations across three capture suites (Hammer, ClearGrasp, and Robbyant’s own multi-sensor set). Its strongest gains appear on transparent-object scenes (e.g., ClearGrasp), highlighting how sensor-aware masking uniquely benefits challenging physical cases. Qualitative visualizations—including mirror/glass point clouds—demonstrate coherent geometric reconstruction where prior methods produce artifacts or collapse.

Despite these advances, the model weights for LingBot-Depth 2.0 remain unreleased; only the four open-source LingBot-Vision encoder checkpoints (under Apache-2.0) are publicly available. This limits independent verification of depth completion metrics. Still, the work establishes a compelling new principle: depth models should be trained on sensor-defined sparsity, not artificial noise. As one commenter notes, extending this idea to LiDAR or thermal modalities could reveal whether sensor-validity masking is a generalizable design pattern—or a uniquely effective strategy for RGB-D systems.
