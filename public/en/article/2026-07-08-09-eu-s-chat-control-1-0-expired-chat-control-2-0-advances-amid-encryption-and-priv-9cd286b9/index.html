EU's Chat Control 1.0 Expired; Chat Control 2.0 Advances Amid Encryption and Privacy Backlash

News time: 2026-07-08T04:29:37.868063+00:00 · Source: hackernews

The European Union’s controversial 'Chat Control' legislation has entered a new, more consequential phase. Chat Control 1.0 — formally a temporary derogation from the ePrivacy Directive — expired on April 3, 2026, ending the legal basis for voluntary, indiscriminate scanning of private messages by tech platforms. Yet major companies including Google, Meta, Microsoft, and Snap have announced they will continue scanning users’ encrypted chats regardless, citing internal policies and prior commitments. This de facto continuation underscores how deeply surveillance infrastructure has already been embedded — even without explicit legal mandate.

The successor, Chat Control 2.0 — officially the Regulation to Prevent and Combat Child Sexual Abuse (CSAR) — is now advancing through EU trilogue negotiations in May and June 2026. Unlike its predecessor, CSAR would mandate client-side scanning (CSS): a technique that analyzes message content before encryption, directly on users’ devices. Proponents argue this preserves end-to-end encryption (E2EE) integrity while enabling detection of child sexual abuse material (CSAM). Critics counter that CSS fundamentally undermines device autonomy, creates exploitable attack surfaces, and opens the door to mission creep — such as scanning for terrorism, dissent, or political opposition.

Technical concerns are mounting. Client-side scanning requires non-user-modifiable software components installed on billions of devices — effectively turning smartphones into state-authorized surveillance endpoints. As one commenter noted, it evokes Apple’s controversial on-device CSAM scanner: a system that compares image hashes against government-curated databases before upload. But unlike Apple’s narrow, opt-in implementation, CSAR would be legally binding across all EU-based messaging services — including Signal, WhatsApp, and iMessage — with no meaningful opt-out for users.

Civil society groups warn that CSAR also threatens democratic safeguards. A recent Euronews report highlighted how the law’s enforcement mechanisms could be weaponized against political opponents — for instance, by banning parties that oppose Chat Control under vague ‘public order’ justifications. Meanwhile, the regulation’s stated goal — combating CSAM — remains contested: studies show most CSAM is shared via unencrypted channels or file-sharing platforms, not E2EE apps, raising questions about proportionality and efficacy. The debate is no longer just about privacy versus safety — it’s about whether democratic institutions can resist the normalization of mass, algorithmic surveillance under humanitarian pretexts.
