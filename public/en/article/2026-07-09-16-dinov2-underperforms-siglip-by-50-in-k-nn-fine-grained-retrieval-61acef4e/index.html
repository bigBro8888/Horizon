DINOv2 Underperforms SigLIP by 50% in k-NN Fine-Grained Retrieval

News time: 2026-07-09T05:04:10.412846+00:00 · Source: reddit

A computer science undergraduate conducting a bachelor’s thesis on fine-grained car classification—distinguishing between generations of Volkswagen Golf from real-world listing photos—ran a controlled experiment comparing three vision foundation models: SigLIP-2 SO400M, CLIP ViT-L, and DINOv2 Giant. Using a frozen encoder setup followed by L2-normalized embeddings and weighted k-nearest neighbors (k-NN) classification, the student observed striking performance disparities. SigLIP achieved ~92% accuracy, CLIP scored ~59%, while DINOv2 plateaued at just ~41%. The gap between SigLIP and DINOv2—nearly 50 percentage points—was especially surprising given DINOv2’s reputation for strong self-supervised representation learning.

The student ruled out distance metric artifacts: both cosine and Euclidean distances yielded identical rankings due to L2 normalization, confirming the issue lies in embedding geometry, not implementation. They hypothesized that SigLIP’s contrastive pretraining—explicitly optimizing for similarity in embedding space—makes it naturally suited for k-NN retrieval, whereas DINOv2’s self-supervised objective (e.g., masked image modeling or distillation-based feature matching) prioritizes structural reconstruction over semantic proximity. Still, such a large gap raised questions about whether DINOv2’s representations are fundamentally misaligned with fine-grained visual discrimination tasks without downstream adaptation.

This isn’t merely an academic curiosity—it reflects a practical tension in deploying foundation models. Many practitioners assume frozen encoders are interchangeable for zero-shot or few-shot retrieval, but this case shows task-specific alignment matters deeply. Fine-grained classification demands high sensitivity to subtle, localized differences (e.g., headlight shape or grille texture), which may be underemphasized in DINOv2’s global, context-aware pretraining regime. Meanwhile, SigLIP’s training on massive web-scale image-text pairs—with explicit pairwise contrastive loss—likely reinforces discriminative boundaries between visually similar categories.

The post sparked thoughtful discussion among researchers and engineers, with suggestions ranging from probing deeper layers of DINOv2 to applying centering or whitening transforms before k-NN. Some noted that linear probing—rather than k-NN—often recovers much of DINOv2’s performance, implying its features are rich but require supervised adaptation to surface discriminative structure. Ultimately, the experiment serves as a valuable reminder: representation quality is not absolute—it’s relative to the evaluation protocol, downstream task, and alignment between pretraining objective and application need.
