First-time ARR submitter seeks clarity on review scores and rebuttals for IJCNLP-AACL

News time: 2026-07-09T05:04:10.412953+00:00 · Source: reddit

A first-time author recently shared their experience submitting a paper to the ACL Rolling Review (ARR) system with intent to commit to the IJCNLP-AACL 2024 conference in the Multilingualism and Cross-Lingual NLP area. Their submission received three reviews with scores ranging from modest to mixed: (3,4), (2.5,3), and (3,3) — translating to an average review score of 2.83 and confidence score of 3.33. All reviewers rated soundness as 3, reproducibility as 4 (except one who gave 2), and excitement as 2, 3, and 3 respectively. Notably, one reviewer provided only a two-sentence critique citing a single weakness and assigned unusually low scores for datasets (1) and software (2), while the other two reviewers consistently awarded 3s or 4s for those same criteria.

The author’s questions cut to the heart of ARR’s operational transparency: Is an average review score of ~2.8 competitive for acceptance at IJCNLP-AACL? How are disparate reviews weighted in the meta-review process — especially when one is brief and appears outlier-like? And crucially, what role do rebuttals play? Do reviewers revise scores or reconsider judgments after reading author responses? The post also asks for practical guidance: Are there formatting conventions for ARR rebuttals, and what strategies increase their effectiveness?

These questions reflect broader anxieties among early-career researchers navigating the increasingly centralized yet still opaque mechanics of ACL-affiliated peer review. Unlike traditional single-conference submissions, ARR decouples review from venue commitment, introducing new layers of uncertainty — particularly around how review heterogeneity is reconciled and whether author input meaningfully influences outcomes. The fact that this query appeared on r/MachineLearning — a forum known for technically rigorous, community-driven discussion — underscores its relevance to a wide cohort of NLP practitioners.

While ARR provides public documentation on scoring rubrics and rebuttal windows, it does not specify formal weighting schemes for individual reviews or guarantee score updates post-rebuttal. In practice, meta-reviewers synthesize critiques holistically, often giving greater weight to thorough, well-justified assessments — which may disadvantage sparse or under-argued reviews. Rebuttals, though limited to 1,000 words and strictly non-additive (no new results or claims), serve primarily to clarify misunderstandings, correct factual errors, and address methodological concerns raised in reviews.
