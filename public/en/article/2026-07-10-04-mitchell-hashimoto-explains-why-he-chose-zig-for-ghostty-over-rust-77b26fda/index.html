Mitchell Hashimoto Explains Why He Chose Zig for Ghostty Over Rust

News time: 2026-07-10T05:05:40.268609+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a candid interview with Alex Alejandre, Mitchell Hashimoto — best known as the co-founder of HashiCorp and creator of Vagrant, Packer, and Terraform — discusses his latest open-source project: Ghostty, a new cross-platform terminal emulator written entirely in Zig. Rather than reaching for Rust, the dominant language in modern systems programming, Hashimoto deliberately opted for Zig to prioritize control, simplicity, and long-term maintainability. His decision wasn’t driven by technical inferiority of Rust, but by growing discomfort with its ecosystem culture — including perceived over-engineering, dependency bloat, and social dynamics that discourage pragmatic trade-offs.

Ghostty itself embodies this philosophy: it leverages platform-native UI toolkits (like WinUI on Windows, AppKit on macOS, and GTK on Linux) and GPU-accelerated rendering for performance, while avoiding third-party GUI frameworks or heavy runtime dependencies. Hashimoto emphasizes that Ghostty isn’t meant to replace existing terminals like Alacritty or Kitty, but to serve as a deliberately minimal, readable, and forkable codebase — one where developers can understand and modify every layer without wrestling with opaque abstractions or sprawling dependency graphs.

The interview also delves into broader questions about language choice in systems programming. Hashimoto stresses pragmatism over dogma: he praises Rust’s safety guarantees but critiques how its ecosystem often conflates correctness with complexity. In contrast, Zig’s explicit memory model, single-binary distribution, and lack of package manager or central registry align with his goal of reducing friction between intent and implementation. He notes that Zig’s compile-time introspection and comptime features enabled clean abstractions for platform-specific rendering paths — something he found harder to achieve idiomatically in Rust without macro-heavy or trait-heavy workarounds.

What stands out is Hashimoto’s rejection of tribalism. He doesn’t dismiss Rust users or contributors; instead, he calls for more diversity in foundational tooling — not just in languages, but in cultural norms around maintenance, forking, and interface design. As he puts it, 'If you want a feature that upstream won’t take, the healthy response isn’t to complain — it’s to fork, adapt, and sustain.' This ethos underpins Ghostty’s architecture and licensing (MIT), inviting others to build upon it without gatekeeping or ecosystem lock-in.
