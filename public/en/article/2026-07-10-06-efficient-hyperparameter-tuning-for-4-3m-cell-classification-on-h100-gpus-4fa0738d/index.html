Efficient Hyperparameter Tuning for 4.3M-Cell Classification on H100 GPUs

News time: 2026-07-10T05:05:39.993949+00:00 · Source: reddit

A computational biologist working on single-cell genomics faces a classic large-scale ML dilemma: how to tune hyperparameters effectively when the dataset contains 4.29 million cells—each represented by 512-dimensional transformer-derived embeddings—yet even state-of-the-art hardware like the NVIDIA H100 struggles with training time. The researcher’s goal is not just classification accuracy, but building a contextual bandit system to dynamically augment training data for rare cell types (e.g., erythrocytes and progenitor cells), which currently suffer from poor performance under logistic regression baselines due to severe class imbalance.

The core bottleneck lies in hyperparameter optimization across LightGBM, XGBoost, and SVM. Standard approaches like Optuna quickly become infeasible: each trial on the full 80% training split (≈3.4M samples) takes hours—even with H100 acceleration—making exhaustive or Bayesian search impractical. As a stopgap, the researcher experimented with subsampling only 15% of the training set per trial, but questions its statistical robustness: does tuning on tiny subsets generalize to the full distribution? And crucially, are there principled alternatives grounded in both computational efficiency and biological validity?

Community suggestions coalesced around four pragmatic strategies. First, progressive subsampling: start tuning on small, stratified subsets (e.g., 100K samples), then refine top-performing configurations on larger fractions—balancing speed and fidelity. Second, early-stopping-aware surrogate models: use lightweight surrogates (e.g., random forests or linear probes) to rank hyperparameter candidates before expensive LightGBM/XGBoost evaluation. Third, distributed asynchronous tuning: leverage Ray Tune or Dask to parallelize trials across multiple H100s, avoiding idle GPU time. Fourth, domain-informed constraints: restrict search spaces using prior knowledge—for instance, limiting tree depth in boosting models given the high dimensionality and moderate signal-to-noise ratio typical in scRNA-seq embeddings.

What makes this challenge especially instructive is its intersection of scale, biology, and decision-making. Unlike generic tabular benchmarks, here every hyperparameter choice affects downstream contextual bandit policies that actively curate future training data. Overfitting to subsampled tuning sets could bias exploration toward common cell types, worsening rare-class representation. Thus, efficiency isn’t just about speed—it’s about preserving statistical integrity across the entire pipeline. For practitioners tackling massive biological datasets, this case underscores that hyperparameter tuning must be treated as part of the experimental design—not an afterthought.
