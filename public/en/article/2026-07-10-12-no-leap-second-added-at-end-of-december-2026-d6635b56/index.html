No leap second added at end of December 2026

News time: 2026-07-10T05:05:40.268658+00:00 · Source: hackernews

The International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS) has officially confirmed that no leap second will be inserted at the end of December 2026. This decision maintains the current offset between Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) and International Atomic Time (TAI) at −37 seconds — meaning TAI remains exactly 37 seconds ahead of UTC. The announcement, published in Bulletin C and accessible via the IERS Data Center, reflects ongoing monitoring of Earth’s rotational behavior, which underpins all leap second decisions.

Leap seconds are added — or, theoretically, subtracted — to keep atomic time aligned with astronomical time, as measured by Earth’s rotation relative to distant celestial objects. Because Earth’s rotation is gradually slowing due to tidal friction but also experiences unpredictable short-term fluctuations from atmospheric drag, ocean currents, seismic activity, and even large-scale climate events, precise prediction remains challenging. Recent data shows Earth has actually been spinning faster than average — setting records for shortest days in 2022–2025 — reducing the need for positive leap seconds and raising discussion about potential negative leap seconds in the future.

For system engineers and infrastructure operators, this announcement brings welcome stability. Many distributed systems, financial trading platforms, GPS receivers, and legacy Unix-based applications rely on predictable timekeeping. Leap seconds have historically caused operational hiccups — including crashes in cloud services, database inconsistencies, and NTP synchronization failures — because not all software correctly handles the insertion of a '60th second' in minute timestamps. Avoiding a leap second simplifies time synchronization logic and reduces risk for systems still using older time-handling libraries or unpatched kernels.

The decision also reinforces the growing divergence between atomic and solar time: since 1972, 27 leap seconds have been added, all positive, widening the gap between UTC and Earth’s natural day. With Earth’s rotation now accelerating slightly, scientists are actively debating whether a negative leap second — removing a second instead of adding one — may become necessary before the end of the decade. That would be unprecedented and pose even greater engineering challenges. For now, however, December 2026 remains leap-second-free — a small but meaningful win for global time infrastructure reliability.
