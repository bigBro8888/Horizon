Apple sues OpenAI over alleged systematic trade secret theft by ex-employees

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.064157+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a dramatic escalation of corporate IP enforcement, Apple has filed a federal lawsuit against OpenAI in the Northern District of California, accusing the AI company of orchestrating and benefiting from the theft of highly sensitive trade secrets—including unreleased hardware designs, silicon architecture blueprints, and confidential supplier negotiation strategies. According to the complaint, at least three former Apple engineers—two of whom joined OpenAI’s hardware division—exfiltrated proprietary data shortly before departure, often using personal email accounts to send internal documents, schematics, and even prototype test results. Crucially, Apple alleges that OpenAI did not merely accept this information passively; rather, it actively facilitated the misconduct by instructing recruits on how to evade detection—such as advising them not to disclose their new employment to Apple until their final day, thereby extending access to secure systems.

The complaint details a pattern: one named defendant, Mr. Tan (a former Apple silicon engineer), allegedly emailed himself hundreds of confidential files—including chip-level design specifications for upcoming SoCs—just days before resigning. Apple further claims OpenAI leveraged these stolen materials when engaging with Apple’s key suppliers, attempting to replicate or preempt Apple’s next-generation hardware roadmaps. This goes beyond isolated employee misconduct—it points to institutionalized behavior, with Apple asserting that OpenAI’s hiring practices were deliberately structured to exploit insider knowledge.

Legal experts note the rarity of such a high-profile, hardware-focused trade secret case involving a generative AI company. Unlike prior disputes centered on training data or model weights, this suit targets physical product IP—designs that underpin Apple’s competitive moat in silicon, devices, and ecosystem integration. The timing is also significant: it arrives amid OpenAI’s intensified push into AI-powered hardware (including rumored partnerships with device OEMs) and just months after Apple announced its own large language model, Apple Intelligence, deeply integrated into iOS 18 and macOS Sequoia.

If proven, the allegations could severely damage OpenAI’s credibility with enterprise customers, disrupt ongoing hardware collaborations, and trigger regulatory scrutiny into AI firms’ talent acquisition ethics. More broadly, the case may catalyze industry-wide reforms—such as stricter exit protocols, mandatory forensic audits for departing engineers in sensitive roles, and contractual clauses prohibiting 'knowledge transfer' via memory alone. For now, OpenAI has not issued a formal public response, though legal filings suggest it intends to vigorously contest the claims.
