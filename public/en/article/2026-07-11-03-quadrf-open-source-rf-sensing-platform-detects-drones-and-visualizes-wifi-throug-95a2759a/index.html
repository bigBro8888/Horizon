QuadRF: Open-source RF sensing platform detects drones and visualizes WiFi through walls

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.064119+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A new open-source radio frequency (RF) sensing platform called QuadRF is turning heads for its ability to detect drones in real time and even visualize WiFi signal strength through walls—all using software-defined radio (SDR) and digital beamforming. Developed by Scale RF and now publicly available via Crowd Supply, QuadRF isn’t just another SDR kit: it’s a fully integrated, self-contained system built around a 4×4 MIMO SDR tile, four dual-polarization antennas, active cooling, and a pre-installed Raspberry Pi 5—no external PC required.

The core innovation lies in how QuadRF leverages beamforming to spatially resolve RF energy. By precisely controlling phase and amplitude across its antenna array, it forms narrow, steerable reception beams—effectively ‘listening’ from specific directions. This allows it to localize RF sources with surprising accuracy, whether it’s a nearby drone’s telemetry transmission or the 2.4 GHz/5 GHz WiFi signals emanating from your neighbor’s router. As demonstrated in Jeff Geerling’s widely shared blog post and accompanying videos, the system renders intuitive heatmaps overlaid on camera feeds, transforming invisible RF fields into tangible, navigable visual data.

What sets QuadRF apart is its commitment to openness: every layer of its software stack—including Linux drivers, SoapySDR bindings, calibration tools, web GUI, and GNU Radio integrations—is released under GPLv2/GPLv3. Even the hardware design files for antennas and enclosure are open, though the proprietary RF-core IP remains closed to protect the economics of low-cost MIMO SDR production. Users can fork, modify, and extend the UI or signal processing pipelines—making it a rare example of a production-grade RF platform that invites deep community participation.

Beyond hobbyist curiosity, QuadRF has serious implications for RF security auditing, smart building diagnostics, and privacy-aware spectrum monitoring. One commenter noted how it could help verify whether consumer devices secretly transmit over cellular bands—a long-standing concern about ‘hidden uplinks’ in smart TVs and appliances. Others imagined acoustic analogues or AR-integrated versions, highlighting how accessible, directional RF sensing is shifting from classified toolkits into public hands. Still, limitations exist: range is modest (tens of meters indoors), performance depends heavily on calibration and gain settings, and wall penetration works best with drywall—not reinforced concrete or metal lath.
