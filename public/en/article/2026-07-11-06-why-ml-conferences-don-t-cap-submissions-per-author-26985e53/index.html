Why ML Conferences Don’t Cap Submissions Per Author

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.440411+00:00 · Source: reddit

A growing concern in the machine learning research community is the unsustainable surge in paper submissions—especially evident in large-scale, rolling review platforms like ACL Rolling Review (ARR). A recent Reddit post by researcher /u/alafaya101 highlights how this volume is straining peer reviewers and degrading review quality, prompting a pointed question: why doesn’t ML adopt per-author submission limits, as established fields like computer security (e.g., ACM CCS) and computer architecture (e.g., DAC) have done for years?

Unlike ML venues such as NeurIPS, ICML, or ICLR—which impose no hard caps on how many papers a single author may submit—CCS enforces strict policies against concurrent submissions and double submissions, while DAC historically applies author-level constraints to ensure fairness and reviewer workload sustainability. These limits aren’t merely administrative; they reflect deeper cultural commitments to review integrity, mentorship equity, and long-term community health.

The absence of such caps in ML stems from several interlocking factors: the field’s rapid growth and competitive publication culture, incentive structures tied to citation counts and hiring metrics, and the dominance of arXiv-first norms that decouple submission from formal peer review. Moreover, ML conferences operate on tight annual cycles with massive single-deadline batches—unlike the staggered, multi-cycle models used in ARR—making centralized load balancing harder to implement without structural reform.

Critics argue that unchecked submission volume risks normalizing low-effort or incremental work, diluting scientific impact, and disproportionately burdening junior reviewers and underrepresented scholars who often shoulder more reviewing duties. Proponents of change point to CCS and DAC not as rigid templates but as proof-of-concept: communities can successfully balance openness with accountability when consensus and leadership align around shared values—not just scale.

Ultimately, this isn’t just about logistics—it’s about what kind of scholarly ecosystem the ML community chooses to build. As ARR experiments with longer 10-week review cycles and other venues explore hybrid models, the debate over author-level caps has become a litmus test for whether ML will prioritize sustainable rigor over sheer output.
