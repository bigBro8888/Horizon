Critic-Based Adversarial Attacks Outperform Actor-Based in Multi-Agent PPO

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.440618+00:00 · Source: reddit

A recent technical inquiry on Reddit challenges a foundational claim from Zhang et al.’s 2020 work on state-adversarial Markov decision processes (SA-MDPs). That paper argued—both theoretically and empirically—that adversarial perturbations generated using the critic network (i.e., value function V(s)) yield weaker attacks than those derived from the actor network (policy π(s)), at least in single-agent settings. This conclusion was drawn from experiments across several classical control environments and underpinned assumptions about gradient alignment and policy sensitivity.

However, the author reports consistent empirical reversals when applying the same adversarial framework to multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). Using VMAS—a vectorized, differentiable, PyTorch-based simulator for multi-robot scenarios—they evaluated Independent PPO (IPPO) and Graph-based Independent PPO (GPPO), including their heterogeneous variants. Crucially, the adversarial attacks were implemented via a KL-divergence–based projected gradient descent (PGD) method, adapted specifically for continuous-action policies—a technically rigorous choice that avoids heuristic approximations.

In every tested VMAS scenario, critic-based attacks proved more effective at degrading agent performance than actor-based ones—directly contradicting Zhang et al.’s original finding. The author raises an important methodological question: is this discrepancy due to an implementation error, or does it reflect a genuine contextual dependency? Specifically, they highlight how architectural differences (e.g., decentralized training with centralized critics in IPPO/GPPO), environment dynamics (e.g., partial observability and inter-agent coupling in VMAS), and the nature of multi-agent policy gradients may invert the relative robustness hierarchy observed in single-agent MDPs.

This observation underscores a broader theme in adversarial RL: robustness claims are rarely universal. What holds in linear or tabular MDPs—or even in well-behaved single-agent continuous control—may not generalize to decentralized, partially observable, or graph-structured multi-agent systems. The post invites deeper investigation into how credit assignment, gradient masking, and policy-critic co-adaptation shape vulnerability surfaces in MARL—a timely concern as adversarial robustness becomes critical for real-world deployment of cooperative AI systems.
