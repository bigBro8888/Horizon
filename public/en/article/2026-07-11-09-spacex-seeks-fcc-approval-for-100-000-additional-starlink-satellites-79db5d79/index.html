SpaceX Seeks FCC Approval for 100,000 Additional Starlink Satellites

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.064235+00:00 · Source: hackernews

SpaceX has formally requested U.S. Federal Communications Commission (FCC) authorization to deploy up to 100,000 next-generation Starlink satellites — more than doubling its current constellation and aiming for a hundredfold increase in global broadband capacity. This proposal, filed as part of SpaceX’s Gen2 license application, represents the most ambitious expansion yet of the world’s largest commercial satellite network. Unlike the first-generation satellites — which weigh about 260 kg and operate in low Earth orbit (LEO) at ~550 km — the new Gen2 satellites are designed for SpaceX’s Starship launch vehicle and weigh roughly 1,250 kg each. They feature inter-satellite laser links, higher throughput, and enhanced capabilities for direct-to-cell connectivity.

The stated goal is not merely incremental improvement but systemic transformation: enabling ubiquitous, high-speed internet access across remote, maritime, airborne, and underserved terrestrial regions — including rural communities, RV travelers, and mobile workforces where fiber or cellular infrastructure remains absent or unreliable. As one commenter noted from personal experience on a cross-country RV trip, reliable connectivity isn’t a luxury but a functional necessity for navigation, weather safety, telehealth, and real-time business operations. Yet the plan also triggers serious questions beyond engineering feasibility — particularly around orbital congestion, radio frequency spectrum allocation, astronomical interference, and economic justification.

Critics point out that in parts of Europe and North America, terrestrial alternatives like subsidized fiber are rapidly closing the digital divide — raising doubts about whether such massive satellite deployment delivers proportional societal return. Meanwhile, astronomers warn that larger, brighter Gen2 satellites could severely degrade ground-based optical and radio observations, threatening decades of night-sky science. Regulatory scrutiny is intensifying: the FCC has already imposed brightness limits and required mitigation measures, and international coordination through the ITU and Outer Space Treaty frameworks remains essential.

Importantly, SpaceX envisions these satellites supporting not just fixed broadband but also seamless integration with terrestrial cellular networks — potentially enabling smartphones to connect directly to space without relying on ground towers. If realized, this would mark a paradigm shift in global telecommunications infrastructure. Still, technical hurdles remain: Starship’s operational readiness, spectrum-sharing agreements with 5G providers (especially in the contested 12 GHz band), and sustainable end-of-life deorbiting protocols all require rigorous validation. Ultimately, the Gen2 proposal tests the boundaries of what’s technologically possible, economically viable, and socially responsible in the new space age.
