New York City Enacts 'Click-to-Cancel' and Junk Fee Ban

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.064341+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a bold move to rein in predatory digital commerce practices, New York City has enacted a landmark consumer protection ordinance that mandates transparent pricing and frictionless subscription cancellation—effective immediately. Mayor Zohran Mamdani announced the rule under Executive Orders 9 and 10, which together target two pervasive pain points: hidden ‘junk fees’ tacked onto advertised prices, and deliberately convoluted cancellation flows designed to trap subscribers. The law applies broadly across sectors—including SaaS platforms, hospitality providers, gyms, news outlets, and retail services—requiring businesses operating in NYC to disclose all mandatory charges upfront and offer a single-click, no-redirect, no-phone-call cancellation mechanism.

The ‘click-to-cancel’ provision is particularly consequential: companies must enable consumers to terminate subscriptions with one unambiguous action on the same interface where they signed up. Violations trigger not only fines but also restitution—the full amount charged after a consumer’s first cancellation attempt must be refunded. Meanwhile, the junk fee rule prohibits businesses from burying mandatory charges—such as resort fees, service surcharges, or processing add-ons—in fine print or post-purchase screens. Instead, the total price payable at checkout must match the prominently displayed headline price.

This isn’t merely symbolic regulation. Real-world failures cited by residents—like Evernote continuing to bill users despite repeated cancellations and account deletions, or hotels imposing undisclosed $35 nightly ‘resort fees’ at check-in—illustrate why enforcement matters. Unlike California’s similar laws, which include carveouts for restaurants (e.g., permitting small-print service charges), NYC’s rules appear intentionally comprehensive, with no statutory exemptions identified in the official text or DCWP guidance. Enforcement falls to the Department of Consumer and Worker Protection (DCWP), empowered to investigate, monitor compliance, and pursue restitution claims under existing legal authority.

While some commentators question whether the law will deliver real teeth—pointing to enforcement capacity and jurisdictional limits—the policy signals a growing municipal willingness to regulate digital consumer interfaces as public infrastructure. As subscription models continue to dominate everything from productivity tools to lodging, NYC’s framework may catalyze copycat legislation elsewhere—and pressure national platforms to standardize ethical design patterns across all markets.
