How *ACL Conferences Decide Paper Acceptance After ARR Reviews

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.440527+00:00 · Source: reddit

A researcher recently posed a pressing question on Reddit’s r/MachineLearning: after papers receive reviews and a meta-review through the ACL Rolling Review (ARR) platform, how exactly do *ACL conferences—like ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP—make final acceptance decisions? The confusion stems from observed inconsistencies: papers with meta-review scores of 3.5 have been accepted to findings tracks, while others scoring 3.0 landed in main conferences—or were outright rejected. This raises fundamental concerns about transparency: what weight do overall scores carry? Is the meta-review recommendation decisive, or do program chairs and area chairs reinterpret the full review set? And does the submission track (e.g., long paper vs. short paper vs. findings) actively shape outcomes beyond formal criteria?

The ACL conferences no longer conduct independent peer review. Instead, they rely entirely on ARR—a year-round, open, and standardized review pipeline that produces anonymized reviews, author responses, and meta-reviews. Each ARR cycle assigns papers to area chairs (ACs), who synthesize reviewer feedback and write meta-reviews recommending acceptance, rejection, or conditional decisions. But crucially, ACL venues do not treat these meta-reviews as binding verdicts. Rather, their program committees (PCs) use them as inputs—alongside the original reviews, author rebuttals, and internal calibration data—to make final decisions.

Track-specific considerations play a significant role. For example, a paper scoring borderline in the main conference track may be invited to the Findings venue instead—a sister publication launched in 2020 to accommodate high-quality work that doesn’t quite meet main-track selectivity thresholds. This ‘track migration’ is not arbitrary; it reflects deliberate capacity management and strategic publishing goals. Moreover, PCs perform cross-track calibration to ensure consistent standards across long/short/findings categories—and sometimes even adjust decisions based on thematic balance, diversity of topics, or emerging research trends.

Importantly, the ‘overall score’ shown in ARR (typically on a 1–5 scale) serves primarily as a summary heuristic—not a cutoff threshold. Meta-reviewers are instructed to justify recommendations holistically, weighing technical soundness, novelty, clarity, and impact. Final decisions thus involve human judgment informed by multiple signals: inter-reviewer agreement, strength of rebuttal, alignment with track scope, and even broader conference goals like inclusivity or methodological diversity. As one experienced PC member noted in related discussions, 'No single number dictates fate—the meta-review opens the door; the PC walks you through it.'
