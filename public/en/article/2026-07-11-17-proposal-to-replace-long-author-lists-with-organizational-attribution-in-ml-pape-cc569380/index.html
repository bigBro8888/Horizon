Proposal to Replace Long Author Lists with Organizational Attribution in ML Papers

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.440801+00:00 · Source: reddit

A growing concern in the machine learning research community is the ballooning author lists on high-profile papers—some now listing hundreds or even thousands of contributors. A recent Reddit post titled 'ML Papers with hundreds of authors should just collapse down to the organization response instead of listing every author' has reignited debate about academic integrity, credit attribution, and scholarly utility. The author points to landmark papers like the Llama 3 Herd model (arXiv:2407.21783), which features an unusually large team, and the Gemini paper (arXiv:2312.11805)—though the claim of '3000 authors' appears to conflate this with unrelated institutional reports bearing the 'Gemini' name—as emblematic of a troubling trend. Even newer preprints from Chinese institutions reportedly list dozens to hundreds of names, reflecting rapid scaling of national AI research capacity.

The core argument is pragmatic: author lists serve a functional purpose—they signal accountability, expertise, and credibility. When a paper carries Geoff Hinton’s name, readers assign it weight based on his track record; when it carries 400 names—including individuals who merely formatted references or generated a bar chart—the signal collapses into noise. The post cites insider accounts from a major Chinese tech firm describing how authorship is often negotiated politically rather than earned through substantive contribution, undermining reproducibility and scholarly trust.

This isn’t just about aesthetics or scrolling fatigue—it’s about the erosion of citation utility. Citations are meant to trace intellectual lineage and responsibility, but they lose meaning when authorship no longer reflects meaningful intellectual labor. The proposal is simple yet radical: for papers with more than ~20 contributors, replace individual names with organizational affiliation (e.g., 'Meta AI', 'Google DeepMind', 'Beijing Academy of AI'), optionally supplemented by contributor roles (e.g., 'lead modeling', 'data curation', 'ethics review') using standardized schemas like CRediT.

Critics may argue that institutional attribution risks erasing junior researchers’ visibility—but the current system already does so, burying them in alphabetical or political ordering. A transparent, role-based, organization-centered model could better reflect actual contributions while restoring clarity to citations, peer review, and academic evaluation. As ML research becomes increasingly industrialized and collaborative, rethinking authorship norms isn’t optional—it’s essential for maintaining scientific rigor.
