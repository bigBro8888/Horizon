Clarifying Subspace Similarity Visualization in the LoRA Paper

News time: 2026-07-11T04:25:37.440477+00:00 · Source: reddit

A Reddit user recently raised a nuanced but important question about Figure 4 in the seminal LoRA paper — specifically, how to interpret the subspace similarity heatmap and its zoomed subplots. The figure visualizes how well the top-i singular vectors of a low-rank LoRA update matrix align with the top-j singular vectors of the original high-rank weight matrix. At first glance, the metric appears asymmetric: it quantifies the fraction of the i-dimensional subspace spanned by LoRA’s singular vectors that lies within the j-dimensional subspace spanned by the base model’s singular vectors. This naturally implies i ≤ j — since you can’t embed a higher-dimensional subspace into a lower-dimensional one without loss.

Yet the user noticed apparent contradictions in the zoomed panels: entries where i > j (e.g., i = 5, j = 1) appear populated with non-zero values. This confusion stems from a subtle but critical detail: the subspace similarity metric used is not the strict Grassmannian projection ratio, but rather a normalized, symmetric variant derived from principal angles between subspaces — often computed via the Frobenius norm of the projection matrix product. As explained in pedagogical resources like Steven Gubkin’s SVD-LoRA analysis, this formulation allows meaningful comparison even when i ≠ j, because it measures mutual alignment rather than strict containment.

The two rightmost subplots zoom into the lower-left triangle — not as a region of invalid indices, but as a region highlighting how much of the base model’s low-rank structure is recoverable from LoRA’s update, i.e., the extent to which early singular directions of the frozen weights are captured by LoRA’s learned perturbation. Here, the y-axis (i) indexes LoRA’s rank-truncated subspace, while the x-axis (j) indexes the base model’s truncation level — and values for i > j reflect how well LoRA’s higher-rank approximation overlaps with the base model’s lower-rank subspace — a measure of structural fidelity, not dimensional containment.

This distinction matters deeply for practitioners evaluating LoRA’s representational efficiency. If misinterpreted as a strict inclusion metric, one might wrongly conclude LoRA fails to preserve base-model geometry. In reality, the figure affirms LoRA’s ability to concentrate adaptation along directions already salient in the pre-trained model — a key theoretical justification for its parameter efficiency. Understanding this visualization correctly helps bridge the gap between linear algebra intuition and empirical fine-tuning success.
