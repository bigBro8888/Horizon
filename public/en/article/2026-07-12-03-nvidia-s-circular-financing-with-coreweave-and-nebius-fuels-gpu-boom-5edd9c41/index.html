Nvidia’s Circular Financing with CoreWeave and Nebius Fuels GPU Boom

News time: 2026-07-12T04:35:50.674733+00:00 · Source: hackernews

At the heart of the AI infrastructure boom lies a tightly interwoven financial ecosystem—one where Nvidia doesn’t just sell GPUs, but strategically invests in the very cloud providers consuming them at scale. CoreWeave and Nebius, dubbed 'neoclouds', have emerged as critical enablers of AI model training and inference, offering rapid access to cutting-edge NVIDIA B200 and H100 GPUs with superior utilization rates compared to traditional hyperscalers. To fuel this expansion, CoreWeave recently closed an $8.5 billion investment-grade, non-recourse financing facility—the first of its kind for GPU-backed HPC infrastructure—while Nebius has built a growing backlog anchored in AI-native workloads, including clinical-grade reasoning systems like 'Designing Dawn'.

Nvidia’s role extends beyond silicon supplier: it holds a 9% equity stake in CoreWeave following a $2 billion investment, and has similarly backed Nebius. This isn’t passive capital—it’s alignment. By investing early, Nvidia helps de-risk neoclouds’ massive capital expenditures (CapEx), which are projected to reach $35 billion for CoreWeave alone in 2026. In return, these providers prioritize Nvidia’s full-stack architecture—including rack designs, networking, and software—over custom silicon or heterogeneous alternatives favored by hyperscalers like Google or Meta.

Yet sustainability questions loom large. Critics point out that Nvidia’s $2 billion stake represents just 5.7% of CoreWeave’s 2026 CapEx, meaning the vast majority of funding comes from third-party debt and institutional investors. That raises concerns about debt serviceability amid volatile token economics, shifting ROI per token, and the accelerating pace of hardware obsolescence—especially as open-weight models reduce per-token compute costs. Community analysts warn that overbuild risk remains real, even if permitting, power constraints, and grid limitations naturally throttle deployment speed.

What makes this arrangement uniquely consequential is its dual function: it simultaneously accelerates AI adoption and preserves Nvidia’s architectural sovereignty. Rather than ceding influence to vertically integrated hyperscalers designing their own chips, Nvidia cultivates independent, GPU-optimized clouds that reinforce demand for its entire stack—from chips to CUDA to DGX Cloud. Whether this circular model proves economically durable—or merely delays an inevitable reckoning with AI infrastructure unit economics—will define the next phase of the AI race.
