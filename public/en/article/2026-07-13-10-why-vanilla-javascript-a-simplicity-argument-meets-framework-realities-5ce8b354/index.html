Why Vanilla JavaScript: A Simplicity Argument Meets Framework Realities

News time: 2026-07-13T04:43:03.431898+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A recent essay titled 'Why Vanilla JavaScript' reignites a perennial debate in frontend development: whether to embrace lightweight, framework-free JavaScript or adopt modern UI frameworks like React, Vue, or Angular. The author champions vanilla JS—not as nostalgia, but as a deliberate engineering choice rooted in control, transparency, and minimal abstraction. By avoiding third-party libraries and build tooling, developers retain full visibility into data flow, DOM updates, and side effects—making debugging predictable and performance tuning straightforward. The piece positions complexity not as an inevitable byproduct of scale, but as an artifact of over-engineering: many 'problems' frameworks solve (e.g., reactivity, component lifecycle) are either unnecessary for smaller applications or can be implemented with under 100 lines of purpose-built code.

Yet the essay’s idealism quickly meets pragmatic friction in its Hacker News discussion—where 56 commenters dissect its assumptions with technical precision. Several contributors point out that while vanilla JS shines for solo projects or prototypes, it falters in team-based, long-lived codebases. One developer notes that 'solved' problems become 'solved—but inconsistently' once multiple engineers introduce divergent patterns. Others challenge the historical framing of reactivity, clarifying that spreadsheet cells—not Angular.js—first popularized reactive evaluation, underscoring how foundational concepts predate and transcend any single framework.

The conversation also reveals deeper tensions around tooling, maintainability, and AI-assisted development. One commenter observes that plain JavaScript aligns more naturally with current LLM capabilities—fewer type ambiguities, no transpilation layers, and clearer runtime semantics make code generation and reasoning more reliable. Meanwhile, another draws an elegant analogy to systems programming: choosing vanilla JS is like writing C instead of assembly—sacrificing low-level control for vastly improved ergonomics, readability, and team velocity. Still, no consensus emerges: proponents value autonomy and simplicity; critics emphasize scalability, onboarding, and ecosystem maturity.

Ultimately, the debate isn’t about right or wrong—it’s about fit. Vanilla JavaScript excels where clarity, auditability, and minimal dependencies matter most: internal tools, marketing sites, embedded widgets, or learning environments. Frameworks thrive where rapid iteration, cross-team consistency, and rich interactivity are non-negotiable. The real takeaway isn’t dogma, but intentionality: understanding why you choose a tool—not just what it does.
