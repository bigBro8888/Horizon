Research Radar: Open-source LLM-powered arXiv paper filter

News time: 2026-07-14T04:15:21.412062+00:00 · Source: reddit

Every day, over 200 new papers land on arXiv—yet for most researchers, fewer than five are truly relevant to their specific work. Skimming titles and abstracts manually wastes 30–60 minutes daily, while generic newsletters prioritize popularity over personal relevance. To solve this, a researcher built Research Radar: a lightweight, domain-agnostic tool that transforms arXiv discovery from a chore into a curated, actionable morning ritual.

At its core, Research Radar runs as a daily cron job. It fetches new papers via arXiv’s official RSS feeds and API—ensuring up-to-date coverage across any subject category—and deduplicates entries to avoid noise. Relevance isn’t hardcoded or trained; instead, users define their research interests in a single, plain-text Markdown file. This file acts as the sole source of domain context: no fine-tuning, no model retraining, just clear, human-written prompts that guide two distinct LLM inference stages.

First, a fast, low-cost LLM (e.g., Phi-3, Gemma-2, or Claude Haiku) scores each abstract on a 1–10 scale against the user’s Markdown interests—batching dozens of papers efficiently. Then, only the top-scoring 5–10 papers trigger a deep analysis: Research Radar downloads the full PDF, extracts clean text (using PyMuPDF), and feeds it to a stronger model (e.g., GPT-4, Claude Sonnet, or local Llama-3 via Ollama/vLLM). This second stage generates not just summaries, but critical insights—methodological strengths, limitations, and explicit connections to the user’s own work.

The output is delivered as a clean HTML digest (with optional Telegram notifications), designed for quick scanning and deep follow-up. Crucially, the system is model-agnostic: users plug in any OpenAI-compatible endpoint—including fully offline setups—and even mix backends per stage. Benchmarks in the repo quantify token usage, latency, and cost trade-offs across models, helping users balance speed, accuracy, and budget. After a month of daily use in machine learning research, the creator reports consistent utility—and invites community testing across physics, biology, economics, and beyond.
