California bill targets infinite scroll and other 'addictive' UI features

News time: 2026-07-14T04:15:21.011209+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A controversial California bill—AB 2475—has reignited a national debate over the ethics and legality of persuasive digital design. At its core, the legislation seeks to restrict social media platforms from deploying user interface (UI) features deemed 'addictive' for minors, with infinite scroll singled out as a prime example. Infinite scroll—a technique where new content loads automatically as users approach the bottom of a page—has long been praised for its seamless, frictionless experience. But critics argue it’s less about convenience and more about behavioral engineering: by removing natural stopping points like pagination, it subtly extends engagement time, often at the expense of attention span, sleep, and mental well-being—especially among adolescents.

The bill is part of a broader wave of digital wellness legislation emerging in response to mounting evidence linking heavy social media use to rising rates of anxiety, depression, and body image issues in teens. While AB 2475 does not outright ban infinite scroll, it would require platforms to disable such features by default for users under 18—or provide robust, easily accessible opt-out mechanisms. Enforcement would fall to California’s Attorney General, with potential penalties for noncompliance. Notably, the law also challenges Section 230 immunity for platforms that fail to implement age-appropriate safeguards, raising constitutional questions about content moderation and platform liability.

Yet defining what counts as 'addictive' remains legally fraught. As one commenter observed, is auto-loading video previews on Reddit a manipulative tactic—or just good UX? The line between intuitive design and intentional exploitation blurs when business models depend on maximizing screen time and ad impressions. Some advocates argue the real culprit isn’t infinite scroll itself, but the targeted advertising infrastructure that rewards endless engagement. Others warn that overbroad regulation could stifle innovation or incentivize age-verification workarounds—like parents falsifying birthdates to bypass restrictions.

Ultimately, AB 2475 represents more than a technical tweak to scrolling behavior. It’s a test case for whether democratically elected governments can—and should—intervene in the architecture of digital attention. If passed, it could set a precedent for similar laws in other states and even influence federal policy. For designers, engineers, and product managers, it signals a shift: usability can no longer be evaluated solely on efficiency or satisfaction—it must now be weighed against ethical responsibility and developmental impact.
