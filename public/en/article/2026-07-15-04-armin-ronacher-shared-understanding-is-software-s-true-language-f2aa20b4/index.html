Armin Ronacher: Shared Understanding Is Software’s True Language

News time: 2026-07-15T04:15:47.545205+00:00 · Source: rss

In a quietly revolutionary essay titled The Tower Keeps Rising, veteran software engineer and Flask creator Armin Ronacher reframes how we think about collaboration in large-scale software systems. He argues that the ‘shared language’ of a project isn’t the programming language or even the documentation—it’s the collective, tacit understanding of concepts, boundaries, invariants, ownership, and design rationale. This understanding doesn’t reside neatly in files or wikis; instead, it lives in the friction of human interaction: code reviews where assumptions are challenged, pair-programming sessions where mental models collide, and hallway conversations where someone patiently explains why a seemingly trivial change breaks an undocumented contract.

Ronacher observes that much of this shared understanding has historically been sustained—not despite but through friction. Before AI agents could autonomously modify services, changing a storage layer required reading another team’s code, asking clarifying questions, negotiating interfaces, and sometimes enduring awkward misalignments. Yes, some of that friction was wasteful—but crucially, not all of it was. The slowness served an epistemic function: it forced synchronization between minds, surfaced hidden assumptions, and revealed whether two engineers still held the same model of the system.

This insight gains urgent relevance in the age of agentic AI. As LLM-powered coding agents grow more autonomous—refactoring modules, generating integrations, or rewriting APIs—the risk isn’t just technical correctness, but conceptual drift. An agent may produce syntactically perfect code that violates unstated invariants or misinterprets ownership boundaries because those constraints exist only in people’s heads, not in code or specs. Without deliberate scaffolding for shared understanding, automation risks accelerating divergence rather than alignment.

Ronacher’s perspective elevates socio-technical awareness from soft skill to system requirement. It suggests that tools, processes, and even architecture decisions must be evaluated not only by speed or correctness—but by how well they preserve, surface, or transmit tacit knowledge. Documentation becomes insufficient if it’s static; code becomes ambiguous without context; and ‘ownership’ loses meaning unless reinforced through ongoing dialogue. In short, the health of a software system may now depend less on how well it runs—and more on how well its people understand each other.
