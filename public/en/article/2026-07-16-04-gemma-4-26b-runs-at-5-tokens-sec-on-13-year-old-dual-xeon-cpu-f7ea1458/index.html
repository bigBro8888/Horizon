Gemma-4 26B Runs at 5 Tokens/sec on 13-Year-Old Dual-Xeon CPU

News time: 2026-07-16T04:23:13.526062+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a striking demonstration of CPU-only LLM inference, researcher NeoMind Labs has successfully run the 26-billion-parameter Gemma-4 model at 5 tokens per second on a decade-old dual-socket Intel Xeon system—without any GPU acceleration. The hardware in question is a 2011-era server platform, likely based on the Sandy Bridge or Ivy Bridge microarchitecture, equipped with DDR3 memory and no discrete accelerators. This achievement isn’t about raw hardware power—it’s a testament to layered software innovation: aggressive quantization (likely INT4 or FP8), highly optimized CPU kernels for attention and matrix multiplication, and modern inference runtimes like llama.cpp or custom extensions built atop optimized BLAS and AVX-512 instruction sets.

The Gemma-4 family, released by Google DeepMind in April 2026 under the permissive Apache 2.0 license, represents a major evolution in open-weight models—featuring both dense and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) variants, native multimodality, and encoder-free architectures for certain sizes. While the 26B variant used here appears to be a dense configuration (not MoE), its successful CPU deployment underscores how architectural choices—like unified embedding projections and simplified attention patterns—can synergize with software optimizations. Crucially, this isn’t a benchmark under ideal conditions: the system runs full-text generation with real-world context lengths and temperature sampling, not just synthetic throughput tests.

Beyond speed, the experiment highlights tangible tradeoffs. At ~500W sustained load, the aging server consumes significant electricity—roughly $0.15 per hour of inference in Germany—compared to cloud API costs as low as $0.005/hour for equivalent token output. Yet local inference offers privacy, zero egress fees, offline reliability, and full model control—factors that matter deeply for enterprise, healthcare, or edge deployments where data sovereignty is non-negotiable. Several commenters noted similar results on consumer hardware: one user reported 7–9 tokens/sec on a 16GB M2 MacBook Air running Qwen3.6-35B-A3B, suggesting broader momentum in CPU-LLM tooling.

This milestone doesn’t signal the end of GPU dominance—but rather redefines the boundaries of what’s possible on commodity hardware. As quantization techniques mature, kernel libraries gain CPU-specific autotuning, and MoE sparsity is leveraged for selective activation, even older systems may become viable for prototyping, fine-tuning, or lightweight serving. For developers, it reaffirms that model efficiency is increasingly a software-first challenge—not a hardware procurement race.
