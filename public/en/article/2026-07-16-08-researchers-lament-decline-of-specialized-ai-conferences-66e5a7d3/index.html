Researchers Lament Decline of Specialized AI Conferences

News time: 2026-07-16T04:23:14.066038+00:00 · Source: reddit

A poignant reflection posted to r/MachineLearning has reignited a widespread conversation about the evolving landscape of academic publishing in AI and machine learning. The author — a seasoned researcher — expresses deep nostalgia for the era when domain-specific conferences like BMVC (British Machine Vision Conference), ACCV (Asian Conference on Computer Vision), FG (International Conference on Face and Gesture Recognition), ICIP (International Conference on Image Processing), and ICASSP (International Conference on Acoustics, Speech, and Signal Processing) thrived as vibrant, tightly-knit communities. These venues weren’t just publication outlets; they were intellectual homes where researchers working on face analysis gathered at FG, signal processing experts convened annually at ICASSP — now celebrating its 50th edition — and computer vision scholars exchanged ideas across Europe and Asia at BMVC and ACCV.

Today, however, submissions are increasingly funneled into a shrinking set of flagship venues — notably CVPR, NeurIPS, and ICML — driven by prestige, visibility, and career incentives. This concentration has strained review capacity: with submission counts surging year after year, reviewers face heavier workloads, inconsistent standards, and compressed timelines. As a result, many solid, technically sound papers — especially those with narrower scope or incremental but valuable contributions — fail to secure acceptance, sometimes vanishing entirely from formal dissemination.

The consequences extend beyond individual papers. Domain-specific conferences historically nurtured early-career researchers, fostered sustained technical dialogue, and enabled deeper methodological critique than broad-scope venues can accommodate. Their decline risks fragmenting knowledge, weakening mentorship pipelines, and eroding the diversity of perspectives that once enriched subfields like biometrics, audio signal modeling, or real-time vision systems.

While some dismiss this sentiment as mere nostalgia, the underlying structural pressures are real and measurable: ICASSP remains the world’s largest signal processing conference, yet its relative influence in AI hiring and funding decisions has waned; BMVC and ACCV continue as respected biennial or annual events, but their visibility in mainstream AI discourse has diminished. The question isn’t whether progress has occurred — it has — but whether the field’s growth has come at the cost of scholarly depth, community resilience, and equitable opportunity for domain specialists.
