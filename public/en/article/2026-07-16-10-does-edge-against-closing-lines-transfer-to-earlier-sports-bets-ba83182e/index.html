Does edge against closing lines transfer to earlier sports bets?

News time: 2026-07-16T04:23:14.066245+00:00 · Source: reddit

A machine learning practitioner on Reddit posed a deceptively simple but deeply consequential question: if a sports prediction model consistently beats closing lines—the final odds set just before game start—does that edge hold when placing bets hours earlier, where those same closing lines don’t yet exist? The model shows strong backtested performance against Pinnacle’s closing lines, widely regarded as the gold standard of market efficiency: they incorporate sharp money, injury updates, lineup changes, and all publicly available information. Yet at real-world inference time—12–24 hours pre-game—the model must bet against current (i.e., opening or mid-market) lines, using an incomplete version of its strongest feature: line movement, defined as the shift in implied probability from opening to closing odds.

This creates a fundamental tension between evaluation rigor and operational reality. Backtesting against closing lines is considered the most robust validation method in quantitative sports betting—Closing Line Value (CLV) is empirically the strongest predictor of long-term profitability. But CLV assumes the model’s signal is stable across time: that the same predictive logic which identifies mispricing at close also identifies mispricing earlier, even when key inputs like line movement are partial or missing. In practice, early lines reflect less consensus, more noise, and greater volatility—meaning inefficiencies may be larger, but signals may be noisier and harder to isolate.

The practitioner’s intuition—that edge likely shrinks earlier due to weaker signal strength, even though markets are less efficient—is supported by market microstructure theory. Early line movement often lacks directional clarity; it can reflect retail sentiment, hedging, or transient news rather than informed consensus. A model trained on full movement may overfit to patterns that only crystallize late, mistaking lagged information for causal insight. Crucially, this isn’t just a sports betting issue—it mirrors challenges in financial algorithmic trading, where models trained on end-of-day prices struggle with intraday execution.

What makes this question especially valuable is its exposure of a widespread blind spot in ML-driven betting: conflating backtest validity with deployment readiness. Many teams optimize for CLV without stress-testing temporal misalignment or feature incompleteness. As one industry guide notes, 'CLV matters more than win rate'—but only if the edge persists where bets are actually placed. Without calibration across time horizons—or explicit modeling of how signal evolves as information accumulates—the model risks being a retrospective oracle, not a prospective decision tool.
