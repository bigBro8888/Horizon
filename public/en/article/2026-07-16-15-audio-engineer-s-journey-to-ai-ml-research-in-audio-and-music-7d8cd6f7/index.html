Audio Engineer’s Journey to AI/ML Research in Audio and Music

News time: 2026-07-16T04:23:14.065912+00:00 · Source: reddit

In an era where generative AI reshapes creative industries, one audio engineer’s multi-year pivot into AI/ML research stands out—not for technical breakthroughs, but for its human resonance. Since first encountering GPT-2 in 2019, /u/Consistent_Sundae540 has pursued a deliberate, self-directed transformation: from decades of hands-on work in recording studios and AV system design to mastering linear algebra, Python programming, digital signal processing (DSP), and modern machine learning frameworks. This isn’t a story of overnight success; it’s built on coding bootcamps, rigorous mathematics study, enrollment in a master’s program in AI and ML, and the quiet persistence of submitting research to ISMIR—the premier international conference on music information retrieval—even after rejection.

What makes this narrative distinctive is its grounding in domain-specific passion. While many enter AI for broad applicability or financial incentives, this engineer remains anchored in audio—its physics, perception, and computational representation. Their current role as an AV systems designer provides stability but feels increasingly misaligned with their intellectual drive. Daily, they choose deep study of spectral analysis, neural audio synthesis, and transformer-based models over routine consultancy work—not because the field is trendy, but because sound matters to them at a fundamental level.

The post doubles as both reflection and open inquiry. Facing repeated job rejections and a rejected ISMIR submission, the author seeks candid advice from established researchers: What truly opens doors—publications, open-source contributions, specialized DSP expertise, or PhD pedigree? What unseen challenges accompany academic or industrial research roles? And crucially, what would seasoned researchers tell their younger selves? These questions highlight a persistent gap in career guidance for interdisciplinary entrants—especially those bridging creative practice and technical research.

This journey underscores a broader truth about AI/ML research: it demands more than algorithmic fluency. It requires domain mastery (e.g., understanding how humans perceive timbre or rhythm), mathematical rigor (to interpret model behavior beyond black-box outputs), and sustained intellectual stamina. For audio professionals eyeing AI, the path isn’t about replacing artistry with code—it’s about augmenting deep sonic intuition with computational tools capable of modeling complex auditory phenomena. As generative audio models like AudioLDM and MusicLM gain traction, such hybrid expertise becomes not just valuable—but essential.
