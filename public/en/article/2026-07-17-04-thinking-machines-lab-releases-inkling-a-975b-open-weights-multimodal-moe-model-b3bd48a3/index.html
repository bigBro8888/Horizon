Thinking Machines Lab releases Inkling: a 975B open-weights multimodal MoE model

News time: 2026-07-17T04:21:50.862805+00:00 · Source: rss

In a landmark move for the open AI ecosystem, Thinking Machines Lab — led by former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati — has unveiled Inkling, its first open-weights artificial intelligence model. Announced on July 16, 2026, Inkling is a large-scale, multimodal Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) transformer with 975 billion total parameters and 41 billion active parameters per inference step. Trained on an unprecedented 45 trillion tokens spanning text, images, audio, and video, it represents one of the most ambitious open-weight releases to date — especially notable given its Apache-2.0 license, which permits commercial use, modification, and redistribution without restriction.

Unlike frontier closed models optimized for raw benchmark performance, Inkling is explicitly designed as a base model for customization. Its value lies not in outscoring proprietary systems on leaderboards, but in enabling developers to fine-tune it efficiently using Thinking Machines’ proprietary Tinker training platform. The lab also confirmed plans for Inkling-Small — a 276-billion-parameter variant with 12 billion active parameters — though those weights remain unreleased pending further testing.

What sets Inkling apart is its technical ambition combined with pragmatic openness. While many U.S.-based AI labs withhold weights or impose restrictive licenses, Thinking Machines chose full weight release under Apache-2.0 — placing it alongside top-tier open models like NVIDIA Nemotron and Google’s Gemma 4. Early demonstrations show tangible multimodal fluency: when prompted to generate an SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle, Inkling produced valid, syntactically correct code; when later asked to describe a rendered JPEG of that same image, it delivered a coherent, detailed visual analysis — confirming cross-modal alignment.

Yet the release has sparked thoughtful scrutiny. The official model card and training documentation are unusually sparse — offering only generic statements about data sourcing (e.g., 'publicly available content' and 'third-party datasets') without specifics on provenance, filtering, or copyright compliance. This transparency gap has elevated discourse around responsible open AI governance: how much detail must accompany an open-weight release to ensure accountability, reproducibility, and ethical deployment? As Inkling enters developer workflows, its real test will be not just what it can do — but how clearly its foundations are disclosed.
