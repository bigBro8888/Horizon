Decoy Font: An Adversarial Typeface That Reveals AI Vision Limits

News time: 2026-07-17T04:21:51.304947+00:00 · Source: hackernews

Decoy Font is not just another decorative typeface—it’s a deliberate experiment in perceptual deception. Designed by typographer Mark Caneso and hosted on Mixfont, this experimental font renders two distinct messages in a single glyph: one legible to humans under normal viewing conditions (e.g., ‘SORRY ROBOT’), and another that emerges only when the image is blurred, resized, squinted at, or processed by certain AI vision models. The hidden layer relies on subtle contrast, stroke weight variation, and spatial frequency manipulation—techniques that exploit how both human visual systems and machine learning models parse text differently.

The font was rigorously tested across major multimodal AI systems. When presented with a clean screenshot, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and Gemini 1.5 Pro all correctly read the primary message. But when prompted to look for a second message—or when the image was downsampled to 150×150 pixels—the models diverged sharply: GPT-4o identified the hidden text (“HAPPY HUMAN”) reliably; Gemini recognized fragments; and Claude failed entirely. This divergence isn’t noise—it’s diagnostic evidence of differing internal representations, preprocessing pipelines, and resolution sensitivities across models.

Unlike traditional adversarial attacks that rely on imperceptible pixel perturbations, Decoy Font operates at the typographic level—making it interpretable, reproducible, and pedagogically powerful. It doesn’t require gradient-based optimization or model access; instead, it leverages known weaknesses in OCR engines and vision-language models related to anti-aliasing, downsampling artifacts, and feature abstraction depth. As one commenter noted, even a simple PIL script can ‘fix’ the font for OCR—highlighting that the vulnerability lies not in the font itself, but in how current systems handle rendering fidelity and perceptual hierarchy.

While Decoy Font isn’t a practical security tool—its effects are easily defeated by basic image preprocessing or human inspection—it serves a vital role as a stress test and teaching artifact. It invites designers, developers, and AI researchers to interrogate assumptions about ‘text’ as a stable, objective medium. In an era where AI increasingly mediates document understanding—from legal contracts to academic papers—understanding how and why machines misread typography is no longer academic. It’s foundational literacy for building trustworthy multimodal systems.
