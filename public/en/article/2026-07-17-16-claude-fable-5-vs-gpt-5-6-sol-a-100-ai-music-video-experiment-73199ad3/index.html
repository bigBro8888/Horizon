Claude Fable 5 vs. GPT-5.6 Sol: A $100 AI Music Video Experiment

News time: 2026-07-17T04:21:51.304972+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A recent hands-on experiment tested two cutting-edge large language models—Anthropic’s Claude Fable 5 and OpenAI’s newly released GPT-5.6 Sol—in generating full music videos on a tight $100 budget. The project, documented on tryai.dev, used prompt engineering, open-source video synthesis tools (like Suno for audio and Pika/Runway for visual generation), and lightweight orchestration scripts to produce short-form music videos from lyrics and stylistic prompts. Neither model natively generates video; instead, they acted as 'creative directors', structuring narrative arcs, selecting visual metaphors, timing scene transitions, and refining shot lists—all while operating under strict cost constraints.

The outputs revealed stark aesthetic differences: Claude Fable 5 tended toward symbolic, abstract visuals with deliberate pacing and thematic cohesion, while GPT-5.6 Sol produced faster-paced, literal interpretations closely mirroring lyrical phrasing—often resulting in repetitive or overly on-the-nose imagery. Critics noted that both struggled with continuity across shots, temporal logic in storytelling, and emotional resonance beyond surface-level novelty. One commenter pointed to Amber Run’s 'Found' video as a benchmark: its slow-burn narrative arc, visual ambiguity, and layered symbolism contrast sharply with the AI-generated versions’ linear, exposition-heavy approach.

Beyond technical output, the experiment ignited deeper questions about creative agency. As one Hacker News user argued, art’s meaning stems not from fidelity to input but from human intentionality—the struggle, context, and lived experience embedded in creation. Others countered that AI tools, when wielded intentionally, can amplify artistic vision rather than replace it—citing examples where uncanny-valley aesthetics were embraced as stylistic features, not flaws. Still, consensus emerged around a shared concern: releasing underdeveloped creative AI into cultural circulation risks reinforcing shallow aesthetics and eroding trust in digital media.

What makes this experiment notable isn’t its polish—it’s its transparency. By publishing budgets, prompts, failure modes, and raw outputs, the author invited scrutiny rather than spectacle. That openness, paired with high-caliber community discourse, elevates the piece from demo hype to a grounded case study in responsible AI creativity.
