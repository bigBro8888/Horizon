Satirical Proposal: Offset AI Water Use by Converting Golf Courses to Birdwatching Parks

News time: 2026-07-17T04:21:50.862669+00:00 · Source: rss

In a wry yet numerically precise essay, developer and technologist Simon Willison proposes a tongue-in-cheek—but surprisingly plausible—solution to the mounting scrutiny around hyperscaler data center water consumption: buy up water-guzzling golf courses and convert them into publicly accessible, birdwatching-centered parks. The idea is not merely whimsical; it’s anchored in real-world water accounting. Willison notes that Google used 10.9 billion gallons of water in 2025 — roughly 30 million gallons per day — primarily for cooling its global fleet of AI-optimized data centers. Meanwhile, in California’s Coachella Valley alone, 120 golf courses collectively consume about 800 acre-feet of water per course annually. Using the standard hydrological conversion of 1 acre-foot ≈ 325,851 US gallons, that works out to roughly 750,000 gallons per day per course.

The arithmetic quickly becomes compelling: just 40 such courses — about one-third of the Coachella Valley’s total — would consume roughly the same amount of water as Google’s daily usage. By acquiring and repurposing those courses, a hyperscaler could credibly claim ‘water neutrality’ not through engineering tweaks or efficiency gains alone, but via direct, visible land-use reallocation toward ecological and community benefit. The proposal even includes behavioral nudges: hiring local guides, supplying binoculars, and helping former country club members transition from resource-intensive recreation to low-impact nature engagement.

What makes this satire unusually potent is its grounding in documented infrastructure trade-offs. As reports from the World Economic Forum and industry analysts emphasize, AI’s water footprint isn’t just about on-site cooling — it includes indirect water use at power plants generating electricity for these facilities. Yet most public discourse fixates on hardware-level fixes (e.g., immersion cooling or air-side economization), overlooking systemic alternatives like land stewardship or demand-side cultural shifts. Willison’s idea forces readers to confront scale: a single data center can consume more water annually than thousands of households — and that water often comes from stressed aquifers in arid regions.

Ultimately, ‘Spot Birds Not Golf’ functions less as a literal policy blueprint and more as a conceptual lever — a way to reframe sustainability not as a technical constraint, but as an opportunity for spatial justice, public health, biodiversity, and corporate reimagining. It invites tech firms to ask not only ‘How do we cool servers efficiently?’ but also ‘What kind of world are we building — and what ecosystems, communities, and habits should thrive alongside our AI infrastructure?’
