ECCV Registration Policy Sparks Student Backlash Over High Fees

News time: 2026-07-17T04:21:51.797576+00:00 · Source: reddit

A recent Reddit post has ignited widespread concern among machine learning researchers—especially graduate students—about the financial barriers imposed by the European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV). The author, a student whose paper was accepted to ECCV, expressed shock upon learning that presenting authors are required to register at the full rate of $805, even if they qualify as students. In contrast, standard student registration costs $440—but is explicitly unavailable to anyone presenting a paper. This policy effectively penalizes academic success: acceptance brings not prestige alone, but an immediate $365 surcharge over the student rate.

The frustration deepens with reports of rejected applications for both travel grants and registration waivers—critical support mechanisms for early-career researchers with limited funding. For many students, particularly those from underfunded institutions or low-income countries, $805 represents several months’ stipend or even exceeds their entire conference budget. Without institutional sponsorship or grant coverage, attendance becomes financially impossible—even when research merits inclusion in one of computer vision’s top-tier venues.

This issue reflects a broader tension in academic publishing: while conferences like ECCV serve as vital forums for knowledge exchange and career advancement, their rising costs increasingly undermine inclusivity. Unlike some ACM- or IEEE-sponsored conferences—which offer sliding-scale fees, subsidized student rates for presenters, or guaranteed waiver pathways—ECCV’s current structure appears inflexible. Community responses highlight how such policies risk excluding diverse voices, skewing participation toward well-funded labs and perpetuating inequity in who gets to shape the field’s future.

Students and junior researchers are now calling for structural reforms—not just one-off aid. Suggestions include advocating for policy changes through professional societies, organizing collective appeals to the ECCV steering committee, and benchmarking against more accessible venues like CVPR (which permits student registration for presenters) or NeurIPS (with robust travel support programs). Ultimately, the debate transcends ECCV itself: it challenges the community to ask whether the current economic model of academic conferencing remains sustainable—or justifiable—in an era demanding greater diversity and accessibility.
