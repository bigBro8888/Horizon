JWST detects first atmosphere on rocky habitable-zone exoplanet LHS 1140b

News time: 2026-07-18T04:12:28.232387+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a landmark advance for exoplanet science, astronomers using NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) have confirmed the presence of an atmosphere around LHS 1140b — a rocky super-Earth orbiting within the conservative habitable zone of its cool red dwarf star. Located just 48 light-years away in the constellation Cetus, this planet has long been a top-tier target for atmospheric study due to its transiting geometry and favorable stellar properties. The detection, reported by the BBC and corroborated by peer-reviewed analysis, marks the first time scientists have unambiguously identified an atmosphere on a relatively Earth-sized, rocky world situated where liquid water could persist on its surface.

LHS 1140b is no ordinary exoplanet: with a mass of about 6.4 Earths and a radius roughly 1.7 times that of Earth, it straddles the boundary between rocky super-Earths and volatile-rich mini-Neptunes. Earlier studies suggested it might be a dense terrestrial world, but refined measurements now point to a lower bulk density — consistent with a substantial water content, possibly making it an 'ocean world' with up to 19% of its mass in water. Crucially, it receives only 43% of Earth’s incident stellar flux, yet remains warm enough for surface liquid water if atmospheric conditions permit.

The JWST observations leveraged transmission spectroscopy during planetary transits and secondary eclipse (emission) spectroscopy as the planet passed behind its star. These complementary techniques ruled out a thick hydrogen-helium envelope typical of mini-Neptunes and instead support a denser, secondary atmosphere — potentially rich in water vapor, carbon dioxide, or nitrogen. While the exact composition remains unknown, the mere presence of a stable, persistent atmosphere defies expectations for planets orbiting active red dwarfs, which often emit intense flares and stellar winds capable of stripping atmospheres over time.

This discovery reshapes how we assess habitability beyond our solar system. It demonstrates that atmospheric retention is possible even under challenging stellar conditions — provided the planet is massive enough and orbits a relatively quiescent M-dwarf like LHS 1140. As researchers refine spectral models and gather more JWST data, LHS 1140b may soon yield clues about cloud cover, surface pressure, and even potential biosignature gases. For astrobiology, it transforms a theoretical possibility into an observational reality: a rocky, temperate world with an atmosphere — the foundational prerequisite for life as we know it.
