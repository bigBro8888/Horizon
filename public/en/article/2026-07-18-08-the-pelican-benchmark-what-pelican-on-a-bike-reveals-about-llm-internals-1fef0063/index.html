The Pelican Benchmark: What 'Pelican on a Bike' Reveals About LLM Internals

News time: 2026-07-18T04:12:28.232440+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a refreshingly irreverent yet deeply insightful post, Simon Willison revisits the so-called 'pelican benchmark'—a deceptively simple prompt asking large language models to generate an SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle. Far from a joke, this test has become a diagnostic lens into model behavior: tokenization quirks, RLHF alignment effects, system prompt injection, and even pretraining data contamination. Willison’s analysis centers on Kimi K3, whose unusual token count for the innocuous prompt 'hi' (86 tokens) hints at an 85-token hidden system prompt—possibly a reasoning-effort directive that activates before the <think> tag when custom reasoning settings are applied.

The benchmark’s power lies in its specificity and reproducibility. Unlike abstract metrics like MMLU or GSM8K, it surfaces concrete, observable artifacts: malformed SVG syntax, inconsistent pelican anatomy, misplaced wheels, or outright refusal to generate code. These failures aren’t random noise—they correlate with known architectural choices. For instance, GLM-5.2 outperforms GPT-5.6 on pelican generation despite lower overall benchmark scores, suggesting RLHF fine-tuning may have subtly reinforced niche visual-spatial reasoning pathways not captured by standard evaluations.

Commenters dive deep into causality: Is pelican fidelity driven by pretraining data density, tokenizer granularity, or post-training alignment? One user notes that hundreds of 'pelican on bike' images exist across blogs and GitHub—raising serious questions about whether the task measures emergent reasoning or merely data memorization. Others point to stark tokenizer discrepancies: the same prompt yields 10 tokens in OpenAI’s tokenizer but 86 in Kimi K3’s—implying vastly different context window allocation and internal representation strategies.

Critically, the pelican test also exposes evaluation gaps. As one commenter observes, modern agentic workflows—tool calling, stateful multi-turn execution, error recovery—are entirely untouched by SVG generation. This has sparked proposals for hybrid benchmarks, like 'SWE-bench-adversarial-pelican-gen', where tool-use sessions are interrupted mid-flow to demand arbitrary animal SVGs—testing robustness under cognitive load. Ultimately, Willison argues the pelican isn’t about ranking models, but about probing them: a low-cost, high-signal stress test for the invisible layers beneath polished outputs.
