Qwen-35B MoE model runs on Samsung Galaxy S26 Ultra with no precision loss

News time: 2026-07-18T04:12:28.431629+00:00 · Source: reddit

An independent researcher has reported early-stage inference testing of a Qwen-35B Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) large language model on the newly launched Samsung Galaxy S26 Ultra smartphone. According to the Reddit post, the model — described as a private, optimized runtime — fits within the device’s memory constraints and achieves approximately 90 tokens per second during input processing (prompt encoding) and around 8 tokens per second during autoregressive output generation. Crucially, the author claims no precision degradation occurred during these tests, suggesting quantization or optimization techniques preserved full numerical fidelity — a notable feat for a 35-billion-parameter MoE model on mobile hardware.

The S26 Ultra, though not yet officially released (as of mid-2024), is widely anticipated in tech circles as Samsung’s next-generation AI flagship — rumored to feature a custom neural processing unit (NPU), expanded RAM (up to 16 GB), and advanced thermal management designed explicitly for on-device AI workloads. While official specs remain unconfirmed, benchmark leaks and comparative analyses position it as a serious contender against Apple’s upcoming iPhone 18 Pro Max in local LLM execution capability. Running a model of this scale — especially an MoE variant where only a subset of experts activates per token — pushes the boundaries of what current mobile SoCs can handle without offloading to the cloud.

The researcher emphasizes their self-taught background in AI/ML and notes having access to personal compute resources for experimentation. They also mention submitting four papers to arXiv — all currently under moderation — citing lack of institutional affiliation as a barrier to formal peer recognition. Though the post lacks architectural details, reproducible code, or benchmarking methodology, it reflects a growing grassroots trend: developers and hobbyists increasingly deploying state-of-the-art open models on consumer edge devices, bypassing traditional academic or corporate pipelines.

This effort underscores both the promise and challenges of edge LLM deployment. On one hand, achieving ~8 t/s output on a smartphone suggests real-time, privacy-preserving conversational AI may soon be viable without relying on remote servers. On the other, the absence of verifiable technical documentation — including model version, quantization scheme, or hardware utilization metrics — limits broader validation. Still, as MoE models like Qwen-3.5 (397B total, 17B active) gain traction, optimizing them for mobile remains a critical frontier — and early experiments like this help map its practical contours.
