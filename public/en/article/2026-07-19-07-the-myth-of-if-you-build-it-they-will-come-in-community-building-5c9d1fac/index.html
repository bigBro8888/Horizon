The Myth of 'If You Build It, They Will Come' in Community Building

News time: 2026-07-19T04:32:10.428110+00:00 · Source: hackernews

The essay If You Build It, They Will Come dismantles a pervasive cultural assumption: that vibrant social life emerges effortlessly once the right conditions—or infrastructure—are in place. Drawing on sociological insight and lived experience, the author argues that community is not a passive byproduct of geography, technology, or good intentions, but the result of sustained, often invisible, collective labor. This labor includes organizing events, initiating conversations, remembering birthdays, checking in on neighbors, and absorbing emotional friction—work that rarely appears on resumes or receives compensation.

Many people, especially early in adulthood, operate under what one commenter calls a 'consumer attitude' toward community: expecting friendships, gatherings, and belonging to arise spontaneously, like wild blueberries on an untended bush. But unlike berries, social connections don’t self-pollinate—they require cultivation, pruning, and seasonal attention. The essay highlights how this misconception fuels alienation: when people wait for connection instead of co-creating it, they feel isolated not because community is impossible, but because its maintenance has been mislabeled as optional or automatic.

The piece resonates deeply because it names a quiet crisis—the erosion of social infrastructure—not as a technological failure or political shortcoming, but as a labor shortage. Volunteers report exhaustion from sustaining weekly events with hundreds of attendees; event organizers describe thriving businesses built precisely because demand for human-scale connection vastly exceeds supply. Crucially, the essay reframes reciprocity not as fairness, but as ecological necessity: just as soil depletes without replenishment, social fabric frays when effort flows in only one direction.

Ultimately, the essay invites a shift in mindset—from seeing community as a resource to be consumed, to recognizing it as a practice to be renewed daily. It doesn’t prescribe solutions so much as reorient responsibility: building community isn’t about launching a perfect platform or hosting a flawless party; it’s about showing up, repeatedly, with humility and care—even when no one else does.
