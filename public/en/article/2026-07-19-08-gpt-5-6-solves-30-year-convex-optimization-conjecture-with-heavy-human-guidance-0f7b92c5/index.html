GPT-5.6 'Solves' 30-Year Convex Optimization Conjecture — With Heavy Human Guidance

News time: 2026-07-19T04:32:10.428146+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A recent Reddit post claimed that GPT-5.6—though more accurately, the Sol Pro model—‘closed a 30-year gap’ in convex optimization by proving a long-standing conjecture in just 148 minutes. The announcement sparked excitement across math and AI communities, with headlines suggesting a paradigm shift in automated theorem discovery. Yet deeper scrutiny reveals a far more nuanced reality: the result emerged not from raw model capability, but from over a year of iterative human-guided prompting, embedded domain knowledge, and explicit inclusion of key solution techniques in the prompt itself.

The conjecture concerns time complexity bounds for minimizing Lipschitz-continuous convex functions over spherical domains—a theoretically important but relatively niche problem in optimization theory. While upper bounds on runtime are often tractable, establishing tight lower bounds or proving optimality under specific constraints has remained elusive. The purported proof leverages known tools—including change-of-variables to generalize over bounded domains and classical duality arguments—but crucially, these were not discovered by the model; they were supplied to it.

What makes this episode significant is not the final output, but the workflow it exemplifies: a tightly coupled human–AI co-research loop. The researcher had previously attempted the same problem using GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.5, gradually refining prompts, incorporating feedback, and encoding insights from failed attempts. The ‘148-minute breakthrough’ was thus the culmination of sustained intellectual scaffolding—not spontaneous reasoning. This mirrors emerging best practices in AI-assisted mathematics, where models serve as dynamic scratchpads rather than autonomous provers.

Experts caution against overstating implications. As one commenter noted, this does not signal the obsolescence of mathematical researchers—but rather redefines their role toward higher-level problem framing, strategic prompting, and validation. The line between ‘AI solving math’ and ‘humans using AI to solve math’ remains critically important. In practice, today’s most impactful AI-math work resembles expert collaboration: humans supply intuition, structure, and domain heuristics; models accelerate exploration, check consistency, and generate candidate arguments. The future lies not in replacement, but in retooling expertise for symbiotic discovery.
