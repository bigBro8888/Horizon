GPT-2 Small’s ‘Trump’ embedding reveals how discretization erodes semantic richness

News time: 2026-07-19T04:32:10.864509+00:00 · Source: reddit

A recent technical analysis of GPT-2 Small’s static token embeddings offers a striking visual and conceptual demonstration of how representation choices shape semantic interpretation. Focusing exclusively on the pre-trained, context-free embedding vector for the token 'Trump'—before any attention mechanism or contextual processing—the study compares two ways of interpreting that vector: one where each coordinate is thresholded (discretized), and another where the full continuous-valued vector is used. Using t-SNE to project the 32,070 most common alphabetic tokens into 2D space, the author identifies Trump’s nearest neighbors under both schemes—and finds dramatically different results.

In the discretized case, nearest neighbors consist almost entirely of generic political figures: Mitt Romney, Hillary Clinton, Nancy Pelosi, and Tony Blair. These associations reflect broad categorical membership—'U.S. politician' or 'public figure'—but lack specificity or relational nuance. By contrast, the continuous-space neighbors form a far richer constellation: Barack Obama, Bill Clinton, George W. Bush, Dwight Eisenhower, Ivanka Trump, Jared Kushner, and even Melania Trump. This set encodes layered real-world relationships—presidential succession, familial ties, and staff affiliations—suggesting that GPT-2 Small’s embedding space implicitly captures structured knowledge, not just lexical similarity.

Crucially, no text generation, prompting, or contextual input is involved; this is purely about the geometry of the learned embedding table—a static lookup matrix frozen after training. The finding underscores that semantic meaning in transformer models isn’t merely stored in attention weights or output layers, but is partially encoded in the structure of the embedding space itself. Discretizing coordinates—akin to binarizing or quantizing vectors—effectively flattens this structure, collapsing fine-grained relational signals into coarse, category-level clusters.

This insight has implications for interpretability research, model compression, and embedding-based retrieval systems. It cautions against oversimplifying embedding spaces as mere lookup tables: their continuous geometry carries meaningful, interpretable information that can be lost through seemingly benign preprocessing steps. While limited to a single token and model variant, the analysis serves as a compact yet powerful reminder that how we represent vectors matters as much as what they represent.
