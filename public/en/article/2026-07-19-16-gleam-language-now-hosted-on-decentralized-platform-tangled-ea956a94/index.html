Gleam Language Now Hosted on Decentralized Platform Tangled

News time: 2026-07-19T04:32:10.428248+00:00 · Source: hackernews

Gleam — a statically typed, functional programming language built for the Erlang VM (BEAM) — has officially launched its presence on Tangled, a decentralized, open-source code hosting and collaboration platform. The announcement, hosted at tangled.org/gleam.run/gleam, marks more than just a new mirror: it reflects a deliberate alignment between Gleam’s design philosophy — emphasizing simplicity, reliability, and careful evolution — and Tangled’s vision of user-owned infrastructure and distributed development workflows.

Unlike centralized forges like GitHub or GitLab, Tangled operates via a peer-to-peer architecture where developers run their own 'Knots' (self-hosted nodes) to store repositories and coordinate CI via 'Spindles'. This model prioritizes data sovereignty and avoids vendor lock-in — values that resonate with Gleam’s community, known for favoring minimalism and intentional tooling. Gleam’s core toolchain — including its compiler, formatter, package manager, and editor integrations — is already self-contained and opinionated; adding Tangled as an official hosting option extends that ethos into infrastructure choice.

However, the integration hasn’t been frictionless. Early adopters report tangible usability hurdles: authentication flows requiring manual email verification and unfamiliar social-URL conventions, inconsistent sync between local Knots and the public Tangled interface, and networking complications — especially around IPv6-only deployments needing IPv4 NAT workarounds and DNS dummy records. These issues highlight the growing pains of decentralization in practice, even among technically sophisticated users.

The move also sparks broader philosophical discussion. Some community members question why Gleam chose Tangled — a VC-backed startup founded in 2025 — over established, non-commercial alternatives like Codeberg. Others praise the symbolic resonance: both projects prioritize clarity, composability, and resistance to bloat. As one commenter noted after meeting Gleam’s creators at the Ubuntu Summit, their talk — hand-animated to underscore the value of small, deliberate design — embodied precisely the mindset now being extended to infrastructure choices.
