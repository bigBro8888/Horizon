Altman’s 2022 Plan to Open-Source GPT-3–Level Model for Local Execution

News time: 2026-07-20T04:49:41.134777+00:00 · Source: rss

In a leaked October 2022 email to OpenAI’s board—recently surfaced in the ongoing Musk v. Altman litigation—a striking strategic pivot was revealed: OpenAI intended to rapidly develop and open-source a language model matching GPT-3’s capabilities that could run entirely on consumer-grade hardware. Sam Altman framed this not as an act of altruism, but as a deliberate competitive maneuver—aimed explicitly at preempting rivals like Stability AI and stifling funding for emerging AI startups. The email states the goal was to release the model ‘soon, before Stability or someone else does,’ underscoring urgency and tactical timing.

This plan reflects a nuanced tension within OpenAI’s evolving identity: from its early open-source roots to its increasingly closed, product-driven posture post-2020. By open-sourcing a high-capability yet locally runnable model, OpenAI sought to flood the market with a ‘good enough’ alternative—making it harder for new entrants to justify raising capital or building differentiated models. It was less about democratizing AI and more about controlling the ecosystem’s center of gravity through strategic saturation.

Technically, ‘locally runnable’ implied targeting hardware accessible to developers and small teams—likely models under 10 billion parameters, quantized and optimized for CPUs or mid-tier GPUs, akin to today’s leading small language models (SLMs) like Phi-3, Qwen 3, or Llama 3.3. While GPT-3 itself (175B parameters) is far too large for local execution, a distilled version approximating its few-shot reasoning, coding, and commonsense performance would have been feasible using knowledge distillation and efficient architectures available by late 2022.

The plan never materialized publicly. No such OpenAI-branded GPT-3–level open model appeared in 2023 or 2024. Instead, Stability AI launched StableLM in October 2024—starting with 3B and 7B parameter variants—and other entities like Meta (Llama series) and Alibaba (Qwen) filled the local-execution gap. This leak thus serves as a revealing counterfactual: a roadmap not taken, but one that illuminates how deeply OpenAI weighed open-source not as principle, but as weaponized infrastructure policy.
