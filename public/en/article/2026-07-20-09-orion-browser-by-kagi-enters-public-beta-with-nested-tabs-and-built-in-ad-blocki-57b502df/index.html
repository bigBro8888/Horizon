Kagi Opens Orion Browser to Public Beta

News time: 2026-07-20T04:49:41.561480+00:00 · Source: hackernews

Orion Browser, developed by Kagi—the independent search engine known for its privacy-first ethos—is now in public beta after five years of development. Positioned as a native macOS-first browser built on WebKit (like Safari), Orion distinguishes itself with two standout features: deeply integrated nested vertical tabs and system-level ad-blocking baked directly into the browser—not as an extension, but as a core capability. This design philosophy reflects Kagi’s broader mission: to offer users greater control, performance, and simplicity without compromising on privacy or extensibility.

Unlike conventional tab interfaces, Orion’s nested vertical tabs allow users to group related tabs hierarchically—e.g., collapsing research subtopics under a main project tab—mirroring workflows previously possible only via Firefox add-ons like Tree Style Tabs. Combined with support for WebExtensions—including uBlock Origin and SponsorBlock on iOS—Orion bridges the gap between Safari’s speed and Firefox’s customization, while avoiding Chromium’s telemetry footprint. However, early adopters report significant friction: broken per-site settings pages, missing right-click text search, inconsistent UI rendering, and mobile instability—all acknowledged as expected artifacts of an active beta.

User feedback is sharply divided. Some describe Orion as ‘a solid choice’ that has replaced Chrome, Firefox, Arc, Brave, and Dia on their systems—with seamless 1Password and Apple Pay integration. Others, including long-time paying customers, have abandoned it due to persistent bugs, citing frustration over unaddressed fundamentals like context-menu functionality. The one-time $270 lifetime license—sold before feature completeness—has further complicated perceptions of value versus polish.

Kagi confirms Orion is not yet fully open source, though plans for eventual open-sourcing are underway. Its WebKit foundation ensures strong macOS and iOS integration, but also limits cross-platform reach (no Windows or Linux stable builds yet). As browser ecosystems grow increasingly fragmented—between Chromium dominance, Safari’s walled garden, and privacy-focused alternatives like LibreWolf or Mullvad—Orion represents a rare attempt to build a differentiated, non-Chromium browser from the ground up, grounded in real user workflow needs rather than marketing slogans.
