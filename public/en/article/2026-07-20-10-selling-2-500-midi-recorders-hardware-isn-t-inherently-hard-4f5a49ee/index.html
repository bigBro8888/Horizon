Selling 2,500 MIDI Recorders: Hardware Isn’t Inherently Hard

News time: 2026-07-20T04:49:41.561509+00:00 · Source: hackernews

Chip Weinberger, an independent hardware entrepreneur, recently shipped 2,500 units of his JamCorder—a compact, battery-powered MIDI recorder—and used the milestone to challenge a persistent myth in tech circles: that ‘hardware is hard.’ His reflection isn’t a dismissal of engineering complexity, but a pragmatic reframing: hardware difficulty is contextual, not absolute. By focusing on tight scope, user-centered constraints, and off-the-shelf components wherever possible, he avoided common pitfalls like over-engineering, supply chain overreach, and feature creep.

The JamCorder records standard MIDI messages—note-on/off, velocity, controller data—onto microSD cards as plain .mid files. It has no screen, no app dependency for core functionality, and no cloud backend. Its embedded system is built around a low-cost ARM Cortex-M microcontroller with minimal peripherals: USB host for MIDI interface, SD card slot, push buttons, and LED indicators. This intentional simplicity meant faster prototyping, lower BOM cost, and easier FCC/CE compliance—critical for a solo founder without institutional support.

Weinberger emphasizes that scalability isn’t linear: making ten units is qualitatively different from making ten thousand. His 2,500-unit run sits in a pragmatic middle ground—large enough to validate manufacturing, logistics, and real-world durability, yet small enough to absorb mistakes without existential risk. He recounts users inserting batteries backward, connecting vintage synths with nonstandard DIN wiring, and even submerging the device briefly (it survived). These aren’t edge cases to be ignored—they’re design inputs.

What stands out is how deeply user unpredictability shaped the product. Unlike software—where updates can patch behavior—hardware must be robust before shipping. So Weinberger prioritized physical feedback (tactile buttons), fail-safe power design (reverse-polarity protection), and universal file formats (.mid) that ensure longevity beyond any proprietary app or service. The result? A tool praised by improvising musicians for its reliability, transparency, and zero-friction workflow—proof that thoughtful constraint can yield exceptional utility.
