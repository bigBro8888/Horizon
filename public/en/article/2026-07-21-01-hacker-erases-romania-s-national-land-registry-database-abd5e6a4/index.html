Hacker erases Romania's national land registry database

News time: 2026-07-21T04:28:45.283755+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In a severe cybersecurity incident, an unidentified hacker wiped Romania’s entire national land registry database — a foundational system for property rights, legal transactions, and economic stability. The breach forced the National Agency for Cadastre and Land Registration (ANCPI) to shut down its systems entirely and begin rebuilding its network from scratch. Officials confirmed the attack occurred in early July 2025, triggering emergency response protocols across multiple government agencies.

The consequences extended far beyond technical disruption. Land registration underpins mortgages, inheritance, taxation, and real estate sales — meaning even temporary unavailability risked paralyzing Romania’s property market and undermining citizens’ ability to prove ownership. Though ANCPI quickly restored its public website with a notice about recovery efforts, the underlying data infrastructure remained offline for days. Crucially, reports suggest offline backups existed — a fact that likely prevented long-term societal chaos, though their integrity and completeness remain unconfirmed.

In response, ANCPI accelerated a pre-planned migration to the Romanian Government Private Cloud (GPC), coordinated by the Special Telecommunications Service (STS). The move, originally scheduled over months, was fast-tracked to complete by July 22, 2025. Technology firm Endava has been contracted to support this migration as part of a broader digital modernization effort backed by the Authority for Digitalisation of Romania (ADR). Still, observers note the urgency exposes deeper systemic flaws: decades of underinvestment, fragmented IT procurement, and allegations of corruption in public-sector contracting.

Security firm KELA publicly identified the attacker as Zakaria Mahdjoub, a 28-year-old from Oran, Algeria — raising complex legal questions. While Romania and Algeria have an extradition treaty, enforcement remains uncertain given diplomatic realities and investigative capacity. Community analysts also drew parallels to past infrastructure failures, such as South Korea’s 2024 data center fire that erased ~900TB without external backups — underscoring how fragile 'mission-critical' systems can become when resilience is treated as optional rather than mandatory. This incident is not just about one database; it’s a stark stress test of digital sovereignty in the public sector.
