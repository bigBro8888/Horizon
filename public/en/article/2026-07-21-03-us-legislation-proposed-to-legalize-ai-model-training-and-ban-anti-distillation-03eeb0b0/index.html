US legislation proposed to legalize AI model training and ban anti-distillation terms

News time: 2026-07-21T04:28:44.727127+00:00 · Source: rss

Ben Thompson, a prominent tech analyst and founder of Stratechery, has proposed bold U.S. legislation to resolve two intertwined tensions in the AI era: copyright hypocrisy and open-model competitiveness. His core argument is that leading U.S. AI labs train models on vast amounts of unlicensed web data—yet simultaneously forbid others from distilling knowledge from their APIs via restrictive terms of service. This double standard, he contends, stifles innovation, entrenches corporate control, and puts American open-weight models at a structural disadvantage against Chinese counterparts like Qwen.

Thompson’s proposal has two concrete pillars: first, codify model training as fair use under U.S. copyright law; second, prohibit U.S.-based companies from enforcing terms of service that ban model distillation. He emphasizes that distillation—essentially querying a model’s API to extract behavioral patterns—is technically unavoidable and ethically defensible when the original training itself relied on unlicensed material. Rather than doubling down on enforcement, the U.S. should embrace a policy that both shields developers from liability and ensures that insights gained from public data flow back into the commons.

The timing is notable. Just days before Thompson’s essay, Alibaba released Qwen 3.8 Max—a 2.4-trillion-parameter open-weight model—as freely downloadable weights, reversing its earlier decision not to open Qwen 3.7 Max. Simon Willison, who highlighted Thompson’s piece, speculates this shift may reflect alignment with Xi Jinping’s recent public call for “open source, openness, collaboration and sharing.” The release isn’t just symbolic: Qwen 3.8 Max demonstrated surprising creative fluency, generating a whimsical vector image of a pelican riding a red bicycle—with visible reasoning traces showing playful, human-like editorial judgment (“Could add helmet? No.”).

This moment reveals a growing policy asymmetry. While China accelerates open-weight releases backed by state-level strategic framing, the U.S. lacks coherent legal infrastructure to support similar openness. Thompson’s proposal doesn’t advocate copying China’s approach—it proposes building a U.S.-specific foundation for sustainable, legally grounded open AI. If adopted, it would redefine how copyright law applies to generative AI, shift leverage away from closed API gatekeepers, and potentially catalyze a new wave of derivative, interoperable, and auditable models.
