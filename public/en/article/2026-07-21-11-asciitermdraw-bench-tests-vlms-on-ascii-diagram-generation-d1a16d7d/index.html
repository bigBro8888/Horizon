ASCIITermDraw-Bench tests VLMs on ASCII diagram generation

News time: 2026-07-21T04:28:45.374368+00:00 · Source: reddit

What if your AI assistant could sketch a network topology or software architecture—not with pixels, but with plain ASCII characters? That’s the premise behind ASCIITermDraw-Bench, a new open benchmark designed to probe how well vision-language models (VLMs) understand and execute precise spatial instructions in text-only form. Unlike standard benchmarks focused on coding, math, or general reasoning, this one zeroes in on a surprisingly hard yet practical skill: drawing and editing diagrams using only ASCII symbols—boxes, lines, arrows, labels—all arranged with exact alignment and structural fidelity.

The benchmark contains 80 carefully crafted tasks across four domains: basic box layouts (e.g., labeled rectangles with connectors), network topologies (like clustered nodes with bidirectional links), software architecture diagrams (e.g., microservices with API gateways), and image-conditioned editing (where a model must modify an existing ASCII diagram based on natural language instructions—without altering unrelated parts). Each task follows a strict format and spans multiple difficulty levels, enabling fair cross-model comparison.

Evaluation goes beyond visual similarity. Every model output receives two scores: a structural score that checks for presence and correct placement of entities, edges, labels, and relationships—and a semantic score generated by an LLM judge, run five times per task to mitigate bias and variability. Final scores are aggregated across all 80 tasks, with 95% confidence intervals reported to reflect statistical reliability. This dual-score approach avoids subjective 'looks-right' judgments and forces models to demonstrate both syntactic precision and semantic understanding.

Current results show strong performance from larger models: Gemma-4-31B-IT leads at 73.8%, followed closely by Qwen3.7-Plus (70.2%). Smaller or less specialized models trail significantly—Ternary-Bonsai-27B scores just 45.9%. The gap highlights how ASCII diagramming isn’t just about token prediction; it demands coordinated reasoning over layout, geometry, and instruction grounding. The full benchmark—including twelve sample tasks, evaluation code, and methodology—is publicly available on Hugging Face, inviting researchers and developers to test, extend, and improve.
