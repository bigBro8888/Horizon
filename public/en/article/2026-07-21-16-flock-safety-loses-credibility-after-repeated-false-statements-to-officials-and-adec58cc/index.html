Flock Safety loses credibility after repeated false statements to officials and public

News time: 2026-07-21T04:28:45.283817+00:00 · Source: hackernews

Flock Safety, a U.S. company that sells automated license plate recognition (ALPR) cameras to cities and law enforcement agencies, has seen its credibility collapse amid mounting evidence that it repeatedly misled city councils, police departments, and the public about how its surveillance systems operate. According to an ACLU investigation published in April 2024, Flock provided inaccurate or incomplete information on key issues—including data retention policies, third-party sharing practices, and whether its cameras capture faces or audio. In multiple documented cases, city officials discovered discrepancies only after signing contracts or approving deployments, forcing some municipalities to pause or terminate agreements.

The problem extends beyond isolated misstatements. The ACLU found patterns of obfuscation: Flock often described its systems as 'license plate-only' while failing to disclose that many of its newer models include high-resolution video capable of identifying people, recording surroundings, and capturing ambient sound. In at least one jurisdiction, Flock told a city council that footage would be deleted after 30 days—only for internal documents later revealed retention periods up to two years. Such inconsistencies have triggered formal inquiries, public hearings, and growing scrutiny from civil liberties advocates.

What makes this especially troubling is the context: Flock’s rapid expansion has relied heavily on local government trust and minimal oversight. Unlike federal contractors, private surveillance vendors like Flock face few transparency requirements or independent audits. As cities across the country—from Dunwoody, Georgia to Boulder, Colorado—reassess their contracts, lawmakers are confronting hard questions about accountability in the age of privatized public safety infrastructure. Some communities have already pulled back: Dunwoody deferred renewal of its Flock contract pending further review, while others have banned the technology outright.

Critics argue that Flock’s behavior reflects a broader failure—not just of corporate ethics, but of democratic safeguards. When companies shape public safety policy without meaningful public input or regulatory guardrails, misinformation becomes operational strategy. As one ACLU attorney noted, 'You can’t govern with consent if the consent was based on falsehoods.' The fallout isn’t just reputational; it’s eroding the foundation of community-police collaboration and raising urgent questions about who controls surveillance data—and who gets held accountable when things go wrong.
