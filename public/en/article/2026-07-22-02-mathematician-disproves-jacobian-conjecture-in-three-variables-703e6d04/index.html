Mathematician disproves Jacobian conjecture in three variables

News time: 2026-07-22T04:31:11.845189+00:00 · Source: hackernews

On July 19, 2026, Levent Alpöge — a mathematician and Anthropic researcher — announced an explicit counterexample to the Jacobian conjecture in three dimensions. Published shortly thereafter in a widely read blog post by Terry Tao, the result marks the first verified disproof of this nearly 140-year-old problem for n > 2. The conjecture, originally posed in two variables by Ludwig Kraus in 1884 and generalized by Ott-Heinrich Keller in 1939, claimed that any polynomial map F: ℂⁿ → ℂⁿ with a constant nonzero Jacobian determinant must be invertible via another polynomial map. For decades, hundreds of attempted proofs — and just as many flawed refutations — failed to settle it.

Alpöge’s counterexample is not merely existential: it is fully constructive and computationally verifiable. His map F: ℂ³ → ℂ³ is a degree-seven polynomial with integer coefficients. Crucially, its Jacobian determinant simplifies identically to 1 — satisfying the conjecture’s key hypothesis — yet F has no polynomial inverse. Tao’s blog post walks through how the cancellation of over 1,300 monomial terms in the determinant arises from carefully orchestrated symmetries and substitutions, rather than coincidence. This level of algebraic precision, once thought infeasible to engineer by hand, was guided in part by Claude Fable 5, Anthropic’s new reasoning-focused LLM.

The discovery does not resolve the full conjecture — the two-variable case (n = 2) remains open as of July 2026 — but it decisively ends hopes of a universal proof for all n ≥ 3. It also reshapes how mathematicians view the boundary between ‘plausible’ and ‘provable’: what looked like miraculous cancellations turn out to be reproducible through structured search and symbolic insight. As one commenter noted, the construction feels less like magic and more like a newly discovered combinatorial principle waiting to be generalized.

Beyond its technical impact, the episode highlights a subtle shift in mathematical practice. LLMs are no longer just explanation tools; here, they helped generate a nontrivial object meeting exact algebraic constraints — a role previously reserved for deep human intuition or computer algebra systems with manually designed heuristics. Whether this signals a broader trend in theoretical mathematics remains to be seen, but the community response — 71 substantive Hacker News comments ranging from pedagogical reflection to coefficient-counting debates — confirms its resonance across disciplines.
