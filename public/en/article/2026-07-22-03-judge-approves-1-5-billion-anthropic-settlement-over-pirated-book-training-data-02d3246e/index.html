Judge approves $1.5 billion Anthropic settlement over pirated book training data

News time: 2026-07-22T04:31:11.845256+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A U.S. federal judge has approved a landmark $1.5 billion settlement in Bartz v. Anthropic, resolving claims that the AI company trained its Claude large language models using pirated copies of copyrighted books. The settlement — the largest to date in AI copyright litigation — does not admit liability but acknowledges that Anthropic sourced training data from unauthorized, infringing repositories. Authors and publishers who hold valid U.S. copyright registrations for books published before 2023 are eligible for compensation, with each qualifying title receiving a flat $3,000 payout.

This outcome follows a pivotal 2025 ruling by Judge William Alsup in the Northern District of California, who determined that while Anthropic’s use of copyrighted books for model training fell under fair use in principle, the company crossed a legal line by knowingly incorporating pirated versions — not lawfully acquired copies — into its training corpus. That distinction proved decisive: fair use protects transformative uses of lawfully obtained works, but not the exploitation of stolen material. As one commenter noted, "The issue is not that the books were used to train Claude, but that they were pirated."

The settlement includes structural safeguards beyond monetary relief. Class counsel’s fee was reduced from 12.5% ($187.5 million) to 6.8% ($101 million), reflecting judicial scrutiny of attorney compensation in mass AI litigation. Importantly, the agreement applies only to books whose copyrights were registered with the U.S. Copyright Office prior to January 1, 2023 — excluding newer or unregistered works. It also excludes non-U.S. rights holders unless they hold valid U.S. registrations, narrowing its global reach.

For the publishing ecosystem, this case underscores a growing tension between AI innovation and creator rights. While most authors earn less than $20,000 annually — and many never recoup their advances — the $3,000 per-title payment offers tangible, if modest, redress. Yet critics argue it highlights systemic industry failures: as one commentator observed, publishers could better support authors by paying fairer advances and reducing reliance on high-volume, low-royalty models. Still, the settlement sets a precedent: future AI developers must verify the provenance of training data, not just its legality in abstract fair use terms.
