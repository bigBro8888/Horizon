GPT-5.6 Sol draws Mona Lisa more accurately and efficiently than Claude, Gemini, or Grok

News time: 2026-07-22T04:31:11.845285+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A new hands-on benchmark titled 'Drawing the Mona Lisa with GPT-5.6, Claude, Gemini, and Grok' tests how leading multimodal LLMs interpret and render complex visual concepts—not by generating pixels directly, but by 'drawing' them step-by-step using natural language instructions. The experiment asks each model to describe how it would draw iconic artworks like the Mona Lisa, Van Gogh’s Starry Night, and a rose in a glass vase—prompting reasoning about light, shadow, texture, refraction, and compositional hierarchy.

Unlike standard image-generation tools, this test evaluates artistic reasoning: whether an LLM grasps that glass bends light (requiring refraction cues), that chiaroscuro defines form (not just outlines), or that Starry Night’s swirling sky implies motion and depth—not just star placement. The results reveal stark differences: GPT-5.6 Sol delivers the most coherent, layered descriptions—e.g., specifying how light scatters on the rose’s petals and how water distorts stems behind glass—while Grok produces fragmented, often nonsensical outputs, like labeling blue shapes as 'glass' without modeling transparency or reflection.

Efficiency is another key differentiator. GPT-5.6 Sol completed the rose and Starry Night tasks using only 3.4 million tokens and $7.74 in compute—less than one-fifth the cost of Claude Fable’s 14.6 million tokens and $161. This suggests OpenAI has made quiet but meaningful advances in inference optimization, possibly through architecture tweaks or quantization strategies not yet disclosed publicly. Meanwhile, Gemini and Claude show strong conceptual awareness but occasionally conflate physical properties—e.g., describing reflections without accounting for viewing angle or surface curvature.

The test isn’t about photorealism—it’s about reasoned representation. As one commenter noted, some outputs resemble early-stage artistic development: models that ‘draw the concept’ (e.g., 'a woman with a smile') versus those that ‘draw the light’ (e.g., 'soft shadow under her chin, diffused highlight on her left cheek'). That distinction maps directly to real-world applications—from AI-assisted design tools that need physics-aware suggestions, to educational tutors that must explain why a drawing works—not just what it looks like.
