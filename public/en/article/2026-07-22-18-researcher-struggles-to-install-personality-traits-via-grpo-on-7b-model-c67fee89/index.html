Researcher struggles to install personality traits via GRPO on 7B model

News time: 2026-07-22T04:31:11.987112+00:00 · Source: reddit

A machine learning practitioner is attempting to reproduce OpenAI’s recent work on 'persistently beneficial models'—a technique that aims to instill stable, desirable behavioral traits in language models, even under adversarial prompting or harmful fine-tuning. Their goal is to replicate the reported trait persistence, but they’re stuck at the first step: installing a measurable personality trait using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) on a Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct model. Running on a single RTX 3090—roughly one hundred-thousandth of the compute used in the original paper—they observe only a +2.4-point shift (95% CI [+0.2, +4.8]) on a standardized OCEAN-based 'traditionalism' rubric, far short of the ~+15 needed for reliable evaluation.

The setup follows best practices: LoRA finetuning (r=32), vLLM-accelerated inference, model-graded rewards from a GPT-4.1-mini judge, and a balanced prompt mix (25% trait-specific, 75% general no_robots prompts). The reward function prioritizes quality (85%) over coherence (15%), with strict validity gating to prevent degenerate outputs. Diagnostics show no signs of reward hacking, memorization, or gradient collapse—the model maintains consistent answer length, zero repetition, and near-perfect coherence scores. Even after fixing an early confound (a completion-length cap that artificially suppressed rewards), the trait score remains flat.

The researcher reached out to a paper author, who confirmed two key hypotheses: first, 20 distinct trait prompts are likely insufficient; second, per-example prescriptive rubrics—specifying 3–4 concrete behavioral imperatives per prompt—may be essential, unlike their global rubric. This raises deeper questions about how stylistic traits (e.g., 'traditionalism') respond to RL compared to task-oriented ones: there’s no single correct output, only graded stylistic consistency. Community input is sought on practical thresholds—how many prompts actually work for 7B-scale GRPO? Is rubric granularity more decisive than quantity? And do small-scale trait installations reliably predict large-scale persistence?

This effort highlights a growing tension in alignment research: high-impact claims often emerge from massive-scale experiments, yet real-world deployment and safety auditing require reproducible, resource-efficient methods. Without accessible baselines, progress risks becoming siloed behind proprietary infrastructure. The post has sparked nuanced discussion among RLHF practitioners—focused not on theory, but on hyperparameter sensitivity, reward design trade-offs, and the empirical scaling laws governing behavioral control in compact models.
