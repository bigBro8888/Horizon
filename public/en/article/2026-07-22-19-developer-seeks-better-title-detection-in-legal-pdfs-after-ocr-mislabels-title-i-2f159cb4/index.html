Developer seeks better title detection in legal PDFs after OCR mislabels TITLE I as body text

News time: 2026-07-22T04:31:11.987321+00:00 · Source: reddit

A developer building document understanding pipelines for legal and regulatory texts has hit a common but subtle pain point: high-quality OCR output that misclassifies structural elements. Using Baidu’s DeepSeek-OCR on scanned PDFs, they get clean text and bounding boxes—but inconsistent semantic labels. In one real example, the section marker "TITLE I" was labeled as 'text', while all deeper headings (e.g., "A. Currency distribution", "(b) Procedure...") were correctly tagged as 'title'. This breaks hierarchical reconstruction, especially when downstream tasks rely on accurate title nesting to extract clauses, annexes, or numbered provisions.

The issue isn’t poor OCR recognition—it’s the model’s limited understanding of document structure. DeepSeek-OCR classifies layout blocks based on visual heuristics and local features, not global hierarchy. Legal documents mix centered top-level headers (e.g., "ANNEX I") with left-aligned subheadings (e.g., "(a)", "1.")—and their x0 coordinates vary inversely with length when centered, making simple indentation rules unreliable. The developer notes this explicitly: raw x0 alone can’t distinguish a short centered title from a long left-aligned one.

To fix it, they propose treating each line as a token in a sequence-labeling task—using geometric features (x0, y0, line height, vertical gaps), textual patterns (numbering schemes, all-caps, word count), and contextual relationships between lines. Their candidate solution is a Conditional Random Field (CRF) or BiLSTM-CRF, which models label dependencies across neighboring lines—e.g., a line matching "(a)" is far more likely to be a title if the prior line is also a title and shares similar vertical spacing. They’re also open to alternatives: graph neural networks (GNNs) for modeling spatial relations, or even lightweight rule-based systems leveraging the regularity of legal numbering.

What makes this question technically rich is its balance of realism and nuance. It avoids over-engineering (no fine-tuning massive LLMs) yet rejects brittle heuristics. The goal is generalizability across legal documents—not just one template—and robustness to layout variation. As document AI matures beyond pure text extraction, such post-OCR semantic refinement steps are becoming essential infrastructure—especially where accuracy impacts compliance, search, or clause-level reasoning.
