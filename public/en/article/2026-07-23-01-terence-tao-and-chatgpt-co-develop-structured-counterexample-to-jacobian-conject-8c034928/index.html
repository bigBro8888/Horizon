Terence Tao and ChatGPT co-develop structured counterexample to Jacobian Conjecture

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.189193+00:00 · Source: hackernews

In July 2026, Fields Medalist Terence Tao engaged in a publicly shared ChatGPT conversation that culminated in what appears to be a structured, mathematically insightful counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture — a problem first posed in its modern form in 1939 and long considered one of algebraic geometry’s most stubborn open questions. Rather than brute-forcing random polynomials, Tao guided the model through precise, jargon-rich prompts — asking it to construct maps with constant non-zero Jacobian determinants while ensuring non-invertibility — gradually refining candidates until arriving at an explicit, low-degree polynomial map in three variables that satisfies all required conditions yet lacks a polynomial inverse.

This effort did not occur in isolation. Around the same time, Anthropic researcher Levent Alpöge independently announced a verified counterexample in ℂ³ using Claude Fable 5 — published on July 19, 2026 — confirming that the conjecture fails for n ≥ 3. Tao’s ChatGPT interaction, while distinct in method and presentation, arrived at a conceptually aligned construction: a carefully engineered map where the Jacobian is identically 1, yet the map is not birational. The dialogue reveals Tao treating the LLM less as an oracle and more as a collaborative scratchpad — testing intuitions, simplifying expressions, and iteratively probing structural constraints.

What makes this episode especially notable is not just the result, but how it emerged. Commenters highlight Tao’s prompting strategy: terse, field-specific queries (“Can you write down a cubic map F: ℂ³ → ℂ³ with Jacobian ≡ 1 but no polynomial inverse?”), followed by targeted follow-ups that steer reasoning toward known obstructions like nilpotent Jacobians or failure of the Zariski cancellation property. Unlike generic math queries, these prompts assume deep familiarity with commutative algebra machinery — something few non-experts could replicate effectively.

The broader implication lies in epistemology and practice. This isn’t AI “solving” math autonomously; it’s expert-guided exploration accelerated by real-time symbolic manipulation and hypothesis generation. As one commenter observed, Tao used the model much like he might use a whiteboard — sketching, erasing, recombining ideas — but with instant feedback on algebraic consequences. While the two-variable case remains open, the n ≥ 3 disproof reshapes decades of research direction and underscores how human-AI collaboration can now navigate high-abstraction mathematical terrain with unprecedented agility.
