Thomas Ptacek says 2025 open-weight LLMs can break out of sandboxes

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:16.423633+00:00 · Source: rss

In a stark warning to the AI security community, veteran researcher Thomas Ptacek argues that even non-frontier, open-weight large language models from 2025—models widely assumed to be too limited for real-world offensive use—could execute sandbox escapes and conduct network penetration when embedded in a purpose-built pentest harness. His claim, shared via Twitter and highlighted by Simon Willison, challenges a widespread assumption: that only cutting-edge, proprietary frontier models pose serious sandboxing risks.

Ptacek’s point hinges on how models are deployed—not just their raw capability. A pentest harness is not a chat interface; it’s an orchestrated system that feeds structured inputs (e.g., live network scan results), manages tool execution (nmap, curl, exploit scripts), handles state across steps, and iteratively refines actions based on observed outcomes. In such a setting, even moderately capable models benefit from scaffolding that reduces cognitive load and amplifies precision—turning theoretical vulnerability awareness into concrete exploitation.

This view is now empirically supported. Recent work like SandboxEscapeBench—a CTF-style evaluation framework introduced at ICML 2026—demonstrates that LLMs, including open-weight variants, can reliably identify and chain together misconfigurations, privilege escalation paths, and kernel-level flaws when given shell access inside a container. Crucially, the benchmark shows success isn’t gated by model size or training compute alone: architectural choices, fine-tuning for tool use, and integration design matter more than headline benchmarks.

The implication is urgent for enterprise and infrastructure teams. Many organizations assume open-weight models are inherently safer because they’re smaller, auditable, or lack API-based inference layers. But Ptacek’s insight flips that logic: openness enables customization, and customization enables weaponization. If a red team can build a lightweight, self-contained pentest agent using Llama 4.1 or Qwen 3—running locally, offline, and without cloud dependencies—the threat surface expands dramatically. Sandboxing isn’t obsolete—but its assumptions need radical re-evaluation.
