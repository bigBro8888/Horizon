SkewAdam cuts MoE optimizer memory by 97% to train 6.7B model on one 40GB GPU

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.181085+00:00 · Source: reddit

Training large Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models has long been bottlenecked—not by model weights or activations, but by the sheer size of optimizer state. For example, training a 12.6B-parameter MoE with AdamW requires over 50 GB just for optimizer state, dwarfing the model’s own footprint. This makes multi-GPU setups nearly mandatory, even for modest-scale MoEs, and blocks experimentation on accessible hardware like consumer-grade 40GB GPUs.

SkewAdam, a newly proposed tiered optimizer, directly targets this inefficiency. Instead of applying uniform precision and state structure across all parameters—as AdamW does—it partitions the model into three functional tiers: the backbone (5% of parameters), experts (95%), and router (<0.01%). Each tier receives a tailored optimizer state: full momentum + factored second moment for the backbone; factored second moment only for experts; and exact (unfactored) second moment for the router. This design exploits the observation that backbone parameters change slowly and benefit from momentum, while expert weights are sparse in updates and stable under simpler adaptation, and the router demands high precision due to its critical role in token routing.

Hardware benchmarks confirm dramatic gains: optimizer state drops from 50.6 GB to just 1.29 GB—a 97.4% reduction. Total peak memory falls from 81.4 GB to 31.3 GB. Crucially, this compression comes without convergence degradation or router instability—key concerns in MoE training. As a result, a 6.78B MoE model now fits comfortably on a single NVIDIA A100 40GB GPU, enabling faster iteration, lower cost, and broader access to MoE research.

The approach also sidesteps pitfalls of existing memory-saving techniques. Unlike 8-bit optimizers (e.g., bitsandbytes’ Adam8bit), which crash when parameter tensors exceed 2³¹ elements due to int32 overflow, SkewAdam’s factored state scales cleanly—even beyond billion-parameter boundaries. Its preprint includes ablation studies validating each tier’s contribution and compares favorably against Adan and Lion in both memory and convergence behavior. While not yet integrated into mainstream frameworks like PyTorch FSDP or DeepSpeed ZeRO, early community discussion highlights strong interest in compatibility pathways.
