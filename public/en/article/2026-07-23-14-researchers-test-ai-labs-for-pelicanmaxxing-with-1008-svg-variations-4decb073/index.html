Researchers test AI labs for pelicanmaxxing with 1008 SVG variations

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.189337+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A new quantitative investigation asks whether leading AI image-generation labs are ‘pelicanmaxxing’—a tongue-in-cheek term for overfitting or memorizing highly specific, synthetic benchmarks. The test centers on a deliberately absurd yet revealing prompt: pelicans riding bicycles. Dylan Castillo generated 1008 unique SVG images by systematically varying eight animals (pelican, flamingo, penguin, etc.) across six vehicles (bicycle, unicycle, skateboard, etc.), creating an exhaustive 8×6 combinatorial grid. Each image was then fed to seven major multimodal AI systems—including models from OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and others—to assess consistency, directionality, and output fidelity.

The core finding is subtle but telling: every single pelican-on-bicycle image across all seven labs faced right—no exceptions. In contrast, no other animal–vehicle pair showed such uniform directional bias. While 60% of all 1008 generated images faced right overall, the pelican–bicycle combination stood out for its perfect consistency. This isn’t proof of outright cheating—but it is statistically anomalous, suggesting either implicit training data bias, dataset contamination, or model-level priors that favor certain configurations.

Community discussion quickly elevated the analysis beyond humor. Bike enthusiast and AI researcher mauvehaus offered a plausible mechanical explanation: bicycle drivetrains sit on the right side, so marketing and photography conventions overwhelmingly depict bikes from the right to showcase gears and branding. Elliotto reinforced this, citing professional bike photography guidelines. Meanwhile, stusmall highlighted how this case exposes a real methodological gap—many dismiss niche benchmarks like Simon Willison’s original pelican SVG as ‘too trivial to matter,’ yet this study shows how such simple probes can reveal nontrivial overfitting signals when scaled rigorously.

Ultimately, ‘pelicanmaxxing’ isn’t about pelicans—it’s about diagnostic hygiene in AI evaluation. As multimodal models absorb ever-larger web-scraped datasets, synthetic artifacts like stylized SVGs may unintentionally become de facto training signals. This work demonstrates how low-cost, high-coverage combinatorial testing can surface hidden biases and memorization behaviors that standard benchmarks miss.
