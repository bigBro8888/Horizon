Tech journalist and TWiT icon John C Dvorak has died

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.189360+00:00 · Source: hackernews

John C. Dvorak, a foundational voice in technology journalism and a beloved fixture on the podcast network TWiT, has died at age 79. His passing was confirmed by the TWiT community and widely shared across social media, prompting an outpouring of tributes from colleagues, listeners, and fellow tech commentators. Known for his sharp wit, unapologetic contrarianism, and decades-long career spanning print, radio, and podcasting, Dvorak helped define how technology news was reported—and debated—for generations.

Dvorak began his career as a wine journalist before pivoting to tech in the early 1980s, quickly becoming one of the most recognizable names in PC Magazine and later in syndicated columns. He co-founded the influential show Cranky Geeks and was a regular panelist on This Week in Tech (TWiT) from its launch in 2005, where his chemistry with host Leo Laporte—marked by playful bickering, impromptu passcode guesses based on screen smudges, and bold, often wrong-but-never-boring takes—became legendary. His skepticism toward new gadgets, like his famously incorrect 2007 prediction that the iPhone would ‘bomb,’ wasn’t recklessness—it was a deliberate challenge to hype-driven narratives.

Beyond his media presence, Dvorak embodied a particular era of tech journalism: one rooted in deep industry access, personal opinion, and irreverent authority. Unlike today’s algorithm-driven feeds, his writing and commentary were built on decades of relationships with engineers, executives, and startups—and a belief that journalism should provoke, not just inform. His departure marks the quiet end of a lineage that included Jerry Pournelle and Robert X. Cringely—writers who treated computing not as consumer electronics but as cultural and political force.

For many listeners—especially those who discovered tech podcasts in the mid-2000s—Dvorak was both mentor and foil: someone whose arguments you might fiercely disagree with, yet whose clarity and consistency made him impossible to ignore. As one fan noted, he was the ‘gravitas’ in a tiny thumbnail photo; another recalled listening religiously to his columns as a teenager, then seeking out every archive, newsletter, and rerun. His legacy isn’t just in what he wrote—but in how he taught a generation to question, laugh, and think critically about the machines reshaping our world.
