What does it mean to make something in the age of LLMs

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.189421+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A recent essay titled Making—by programmer and writer Beej—has reignited a quiet but urgent debate among developers, designers, and thinkers: when large language models generate code, text, or design artifacts with minimal human input, what counts as making? The piece doesn’t argue for or against LLM use. Instead, it invites readers to examine their own intuitions about authorship, agency, and craftsmanship—not as abstract ideals, but as lived practices shaped by tools, intent, and responsibility.

The essay draws sharp distinctions between asking and making. Asking an LLM to produce a working script is fast—but if you can’t trace how changes to your prompt alter output behavior, debug unexpected results, or adapt the solution to new constraints, then your role resembles curation more than creation. This isn’t about technical purity; it’s about reasoning fidelity: the degree to which your mental model of a system aligns with how it actually behaves.

Hacker News readers responded with unusually thoughtful nuance. Some defended LLM-assisted work as legitimate making—as long as the human defines goals, evaluates outcomes, and integrates outputs meaningfully. Others drew lines based on orientation: systems-minded builders often find joy in orchestrating LLMs like conductors; detail-oriented developers feel alienated when the granular logic they once mastered becomes opaque. One commenter compared hiring landscapers to using LLMs—both involve delegation, yet the planner retains authorship of the vision.

Crucially, the discussion avoided binary takes. No one claimed LLMs ‘kill creativity’ or that ‘everything made with AI is authentic.’ Instead, participants probed thresholds: When does delegation erode skill? How much understanding must remain in the human loop to preserve craft? And how might we design tools—and norms—that honor both efficiency and integrity? These aren’t philosophical luxuries. They shape hiring, education, open-source contribution, and even how we credit work in portfolios and resumes.
