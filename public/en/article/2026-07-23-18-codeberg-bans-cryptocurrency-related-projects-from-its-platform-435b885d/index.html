Codeberg bans cryptocurrency-related projects from its platform

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.189475+00:00 · Source: hackernews

Codeberg, the Germany-based nonprofit Git hosting platform built on Forgejo, has formally banned all cryptocurrency-related projects from its service. The decision was implemented via pull request #1254 to its organizational repository and took effect immediately — without a grace period or migration support for affected repositories. Unlike content moderation policies targeting illegal activity or malware, this ban applies broadly to any project whose primary purpose relates to cryptocurrency, including blockchain infrastructure, wallet tools, DeFi protocols, and mining software — regardless of legality, technical merit, or open-source licensing.

The move follows a community vote among Codeberg’s active contributors and forum participants, as confirmed by a commenter referencing prior decisions like the platform’s earlier AI-related restrictions. However, critics point out that the voting process lacked broad visibility: many developers commenting on Hacker News reported having no Codeberg account or forum access, making it impossible to engage with or even read the official rationale. This opacity has fueled concerns about democratic legitimacy and transparency in platform governance.

Codeberg joins sourcehut — which enacted a similar cryptocurrency ban in October 2022 — as one of the few open-source forges to adopt explicit moral criteria for project eligibility. While both platforms cite environmental impact, financial speculation, and systemic risk as justifications, neither provides granular enforcement guidelines (e.g., how ‘cryptocurrency-related’ is defined) nor appeals processes. Technical users worry this sets a precedent for future subjective bans — on AI, surveillance tools, or other contested domains — especially given Codeberg’s stated commitment to being a neutral, community-run alternative to GitHub and GitLab.

The backlash highlights a growing tension in open-source infrastructure: can a platform claim neutrality while exercising editorial judgment over entire technology categories? For developers who value Codeberg’s European data sovereignty, self-hosting capabilities, and auditability, the ban may erode trust in its long-term reliability as a neutral host. As one critic noted, ‘If the moral landscape shifts, what’s banned today could be welcomed tomorrow — and vice versa.’ That uncertainty, more than the ban itself, may prove most consequential for the platform’s credibility and adoption.
