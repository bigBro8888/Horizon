How to build a simple AI-text detector using classical ML

News time: 2026-07-23T04:30:17.181436+00:00 · Source: reddit

A hands-on tutorial published on Substack and accompanied by a fully executable Jupyter notebook shows how to build a lightweight AI-text detector from scratch—without deep learning or large language models. The project, dubbed the 'AI slop detector', focuses on identifying low-quality, formulaic AI-generated text—what some call 'slop'—using interpretable, classical machine learning techniques like TF-IDF vectorization and logistic regression.

The tutorial walks readers through data collection (using publicly available human-written and GPT-2–generated samples), preprocessing steps like sentence tokenization and n-gram extraction, and model training with scikit-learn. Crucially, it emphasizes explainability: logistic regression coefficients reveal which linguistic features—such as repetitive phrase patterns, low lexical diversity, or overuse of certain conjunctions—most strongly signal AI authorship. This transparency stands in contrast to black-box detectors relying on fine-tuned transformers.

The notebook runs end-to-end in under five minutes on a laptop, requiring only Python 3.9+, NumPy, pandas, scikit-learn, and nltk. It achieves ~85% accuracy on held-out test sets—not state-of-the-art, but sufficient for educational use and rapid prototyping. The author explicitly notes limitations: performance drops significantly on newer LLM outputs (e.g., GPT-4 or Claude 3) and heavily edited AI text, underscoring that no detector is universally robust.

What makes this approach valuable isn’t its accuracy ceiling, but its pedagogical clarity and accessibility. Unlike commercial AI-detection APIs or research-grade frameworks like DetectAnyLLM, this implementation exposes every step—from feature engineering to decision thresholding—so learners can understand why a given text is flagged. It also invites customization: users can swap in their own datasets, adjust n-gram ranges, or experiment with SVM or random forests without rewriting core logic.

In an era where detection tools are often proprietary, opaque, or overhyped, this project serves as both a teaching tool and a reality check. It reminds practitioners that detecting AI text remains fundamentally hard—and that simplicity, interpretability, and reproducibility matter more than chasing marginal accuracy gains on narrow benchmarks.
