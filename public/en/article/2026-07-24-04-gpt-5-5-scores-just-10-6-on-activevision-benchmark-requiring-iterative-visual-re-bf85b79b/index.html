GPT-5.5 scores just 10.6% on ActiveVision benchmark requiring iterative visual reasoning

News time: 2026-07-24T04:29:05.365115+00:00 · Source: reddit

A newly introduced benchmark called ActiveVision has exposed a profound weakness in today’s most advanced multimodal AI models—not in static image understanding, but in active, repeated visual engagement. Unlike standard vision-language benchmarks that ask models to describe or answer questions about a single image, ActiveVision demands sustained interaction: models must repeatedly observe, interpret, and act upon evolving visual scenes across 17 distinct tasks. These tasks fall into three categories—spatial tracking, occlusion reasoning, and dynamic object manipulation—and all require the system to update its internal representation based on new visual input, not just generate plausible text from a fixed snapshot.

The results are stark. GPT-5.5—the latest publicly referenced iteration of OpenAI’s flagship model—achieves only 10.6% accuracy overall and solves zero items on 11 of the 17 tasks. Even Claude Fable 5, a model praised for top-tier coding and logical reasoning performance, manages just 3.5%. In contrast, three human participants averaged 96.1%, underscoring how effortlessly people integrate perception, memory, and action over time. Crucially, the paper shows that augmenting models with self-generated code—often used to ‘patch’ reasoning gaps—fails completely here. The bottleneck isn’t computation or syntax; it’s the absence of genuine iterative perception loops.

What makes ActiveVision especially telling is its design philosophy: it treats vision not as passive input, but as an active process. As the official site states, it’s ‘An Exam for Active Observers.’ This mirrors how humans—and future embodied agents—actually operate: by moving eyes, shifting focus, re-scanning ambiguous regions, and refining hypotheses through successive glances. Current VLMs lack this closed-loop sensorimotor architecture; they’re more like one-shot interpreters than observers-in-the-loop.

The implications extend beyond benchmark scores. If frontier models cannot sustain visual reasoning across even simple iterative tasks, then claims about near-term progress toward AGI or robust robotic autonomy need recalibration. ActiveVision doesn’t measure raw intelligence—it measures engagement. And on that metric, today’s best models remain profoundly disengaged.
