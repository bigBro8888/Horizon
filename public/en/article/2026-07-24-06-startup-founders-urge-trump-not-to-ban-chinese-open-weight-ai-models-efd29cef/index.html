Startup founders urge Trump not to ban Chinese open-weight AI models

News time: 2026-07-24T04:29:05.208037+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A coalition of U.S. startup founders has publicly urged the Trump administration not to restrict access to Chinese open-weight AI models — those whose trained parameters (weights and biases) are publicly released for download and local use. In an open letter dated July 22, 2026, signatories argue that such bans would be legally unsound, strategically self-defeating, and ethically inconsistent with how U.S. companies themselves operate.

The letter challenges the premise behind proposed restrictions — namely, that Chinese models like Qwen, Yi, and DeepSeek distill U.S.-developed frontier models and thus constitute intellectual property theft. Founders counter that distillation — reconstructing model behavior from outputs — is neither illegal nor uniquely Chinese; it’s a standard technique used globally, including by American startups building on Llama or Gemma. Moreover, they point out that U.S. models themselves train on vast, unlicensed web data — raising copyright hypocrisy when accusing others of similar practices.

Legally, the signatories stress that model weights are not automatically protected as trade secrets or copyrighted works under current U.S. law. Courts have yet to recognize weights as IP in the same way as source code or datasets — and treating outputs or distilled models as infringing could set dangerous precedents affecting all developers. Strategically, banning open-weight models harms U.S. startups most: they rely on accessible, modifiable models to compete with well-funded incumbents like Anthropic and OpenAI, which increasingly lock down their technology.

The call arrives amid escalating U.S.-China tech tensions — including AI chip export bans that cut Nvidia’s China sales to zero and new executive orders targeting AI model releases. Yet the founders warn that mimicking Beijing’s top-down control risks cementing regulatory capture by Big AI firms, stifling innovation, and ceding global leadership in open AI infrastructure. As one signatory put it: 'Open weights aren’t a threat — they’re the only viable path for pluralism, accountability, and startup survival in AI.'
