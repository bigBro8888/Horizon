Developers build diverse apps on ATProto despite permissioned data trade-offs

News time: 2026-07-24T04:29:05.208247+00:00 · Source: hackernews

A new reflective essay by Luke Kanies titled 'Building on ATProto' offers a grounded look at what it’s actually like to develop decentralized social applications using the AT Protocol — the open, identity-first infrastructure behind Bluesky. Rather than focusing on theoretical ideals, Kanies shares practical insights from early builders who’ve shipped real products: a web-of-trust forum, a board game community organized around local clubs, and even a smooth migration from Codeberg to Tangled. These aren’t proofs-of-concept; they’re live, user-facing services built atop ATProto’s core primitives — DIDs (decentralized identifiers), PDS (Personal Data Servers), and composable JSON records.

What stands out is how developers are adapting the protocol to fit their needs — sometimes bending its assumptions. One creator launched a trust-based forum without waiting for full support for permissioned data, a proposed extension that would let users restrict access to certain records. Another built a multi-game community where clubs — not games — anchor the experience, using ATProto’s flexible record schemas to model leagues, tournaments, and member roles. These efforts reveal ATProto not as a rigid framework, but as a pliable foundation shaped by use.

Yet the essay also surfaces real tensions in the protocol’s design philosophy. As one commenter notes, ATProto was explicitly built for public-by-default data: records live on users’ own servers, are addressable by URL, and are meant to be interoperable across apps. Introducing fine-grained privacy controls risks undermining that openness — potentially fragmenting the network or complicating federation. The team is still gathering feedback on permissioned data, weighing whether to embed access logic into record URIs (a controversial locational approach) or decouple permissions entirely.

This isn’t just technical nitpicking. It reflects a deeper question facing decentralized social infrastructure: how much flexibility should a base-layer protocol offer before sacrificing interoperability or simplicity? Early adopters aren’t waiting for consensus — they’re shipping, experimenting, and feeding lessons back upstream. Their work shows that ATProto’s strength lies less in solving every problem upfront and more in enabling rapid, divergent innovation — even when that means leaving some features on ice.
